### **Analysis of Excessive Bail as Prosecutorial Weapon**

The documentation demonstrates how **excessive bail serves as the primary coercive tool** enabling prosecutors to maintain their over 90% plea bargaining rate rather than face the costly endeavor of proving cases at trial.

### **Statistical Reality of Plea Bargaining Dominance**

Current research confirms that **97% of criminal cases** in state courts and **98% of federal cases** end in plea bargains rather than trials[12][13]. This represents a dramatic shift from 30 years ago when 20% of defendants chose trial[14].

### **Bail as Coercive Mechanism**

The defendant's case illustrates how **pretrial detention pressure** drives plea agreements:

- **Financial Coercion**: Bail amounts of $75,000 required posting $7,500 bonds, causing "significant and undue suffering, distress, emotional and psychological trauma, and egregious financial hardship"[1]
- **Time Pressure**: Without posted bail, defendants face "nearly 3 months in Seattle Municipal Jail for charges which no supporting evidence was ever provided"[1]
- **Plea Incentive**: Research confirms defendants are "25 percentage points more likely to plead guilty when" held in pretrial detention[15]

### **Witness Intimidation and Coerced Pleas**

The most egregious example involved **police threatening the defendant's romantic partner** - a widowed single mother - that "she would lose custody of her child and never see her son again if she didn't testify against him"[9]. This required retaining "5 separate attorneys across multiple courts"[9] and ultimately forced **coerced plea deals accepted under duress** to protect the threatened witness[9].

### **Constitutional Standards Systematically Violated**

### **Eighth Amendment Excessive Bail Standards**

Under *Stack v. Boyle* (1951), the Supreme Court established that bail is "excessive" when set "at a figure higher than an amount reasonably calculated to fulfill the purpose of assuring the presence of the defendant"[16]. The Court emphasized that bail "must be set by a court at a sum designed to ensure that goal, and no more"[17].

**Applied to This Case**:

- Bail amounts of $50,000-$100,000 for **non-violent speech offenses**
- Defendant was **lifelong Montana resident** with **no flight risk**
- **No evidence of danger to community** for communication-related charges
- **All charges ultimately dismissed** proving lack of probable cause

The justice system operates on the principle that "defendants who are not a danger to society nor to bailing on their trial hearings are being deprived of their fundamental rights prior to a finding of guilty"[19].
