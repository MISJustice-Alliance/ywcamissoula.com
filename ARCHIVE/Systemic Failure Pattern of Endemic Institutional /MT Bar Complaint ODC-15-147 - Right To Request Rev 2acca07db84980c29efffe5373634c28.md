## I. PROCEDURAL BASIS FOR REQUEST

Pursuant to Montana Rules for Lawyer Disciplinary Enforcement Rule 10C(3) and Rule 14, I hereby submit this timely written request for review of the Office of Disciplinary Counsel's dismissal of my grievance against attorney Bryan C. Tipp, as communicated in ODC's letter dated November 3, 2025. This request is filed within the 30-day deadline specified in your dismissal letter.

I respectfully request that a Review Panel of the Commission on Practice review ODC's determination that "this matter does not warrant disciplinary action" and reverse the dismissal. The basis for this request is set forth below.

**This Updated Request** integrates comprehensive legal analysis that demonstrates how Mr. Tipp's specific advice that filing civil claims could "appear retaliatory" and potentially harm my criminal defense constitutes active misinformation, not passive scope limitation, and establishes multiple ethical violations warranting formal investigation and discipline.

## II. CASE SUMMARY: A CONSTITUTIONAL CRISIS DEMANDING CORRECTION

### The Core Issue ODC Failed to Address

ODC's dismissal fundamentally mischaracterizes this complaint as a "scope of representation" dispute about whether Mr. Tipp was "retained to pursue civil claims." This complaint has never been about scope of representation—a point I made repeatedly in both my initial complaint and my response. Mr. Tipp has attempted to redirect the complaint toward this false narrative to avoid accountability for his actual misconduct.

The actual complaint concerns three distinct ethical violations:

1. **Active Suppression of Critical Information:** Mr. Tipp repeatedly advised my family and me to "give it time" regarding civil action, while knowing that civil rights statutes of limitations were expiring during his representation, yet deliberately failing to ensure I understood the urgency and irreversibility of these deadlines. *Most critically, Mr. Tipp explicitly warned that filing civil claims would "appear retaliatory" and harm my criminal defense*—advice that is legally false and designed to deter constitutional accountability litigation.
2. **Unjustified 18-Month Delay That Benefitted Institutions Over Client:** Mr. Tipp could have—and should have—obtained dismissal of the criminal charges within weeks or months, given that Judge Warren had already ruled my YWCA complaint was protected speech. Instead, he delayed 18 months and ultimately traded my First Amendment rights as a bargaining chip in a deferred prosecution agreement. This outcome validated the prosecution's and YWCA's narrative despite 18 months producing zero evidence supporting E'Lise Chard's extreme claims.
3. **Refusal to Provide Basic Professional Advocacy:** Despite being repeatedly informed of ongoing harassment and civil rights violations by the YWCA, Missoula PD, and their associates—harassment that continued throughout and after his representation—Mr. Tipp characterized himself as "generally disinclined" to provide any advocacy or referrals, leaving me without recourse while constitutional violations continued.

### The Devastating Quantifiable Harm and Institutional Beneficiaries

Mr. Tipp's misconduct caused $6.4–8.4 million in time-barred civil rights claims, including:

- **2015 Edmonds SWAT Excessive Force (42 U.S.C. § 1983):** Statute expired November 2018 during Mr. Tipp's representation
- **2018 First Amendment Retaliation (42 U.S.C. § 1983):** Statute expired May 2021 shortly after his representation
- **Medical Malpractice (Dr. Marta Miranda):** Statute expired 2019–2020 during his representation
- **Prior Attorney Malpractice (Patricia Fulton):** Statute expired 2019–2020 during his representation

However, Mr. Tipp's chosen resolution benefitted different parties entirely:

**Who benefitted from the deferred prosecution agreement:**

- **Prosecution:** Avoided trial embarrassment after 18 months produced no evidence supporting stalking charges
- **YWCA:** My public complaint was removed, silencing institutional accountability
- **E'Lise Chard:** Her extreme, unfounded claims were effectively validated rather than exposed

**Who was harmed:**

- **Elvis Nuno:** Constitutional rights traded away, civil claims time-barred, ongoing harassment unaddressed
- **Public:** Dangerous precedent that constitutional rights are negotiable commodities

### The Standard That Applies

Montana disciplinary precedent establishes 7-month suspensions for attorneys who:

- Fail to file timely appeals
- Miss statute of limitations deadlines
- Fail to investigate case merits
- Fail to communicate adequately with clients

Mr. Tipp's conduct is demonstrably more serious because it involved:

- Multiple statute deadline failures across multiple potential claims
- **Active misinformation ("civil suit will harm defense") rather than mere omission**
- Trading constitutional rights in plea negotiations
- 18-month unjustified delay that benefitted institutions, not client
- Quantified damages exceeding $6 million

## IV. CORRECTING ODC'S FUNDAMENTAL MISCHARACTERIZATIONS

### Mischaracterization #1: "This Is About Whether Tipp Was Retained for Civil Representation"

**ODC's Framing:** "The crux of your grievance is that Mr. Tipp failed to pursue civil claims on your behalf for alleged civil rights violations."

**Reality:** Mr. Tipp was never retained to pursue civil claims. This has never been claimed in any submission. The complaint concerns his:

- Active advice against civil action during critical statute periods
- **Explicit warning that filing civil claims would "appear retaliatory" and harm criminal defense**
- Failure to ensure client understanding of approaching irreversible deadlines
- Refusal to provide referrals to civil rights attorneys
- Deliberate suppression of information about urgent time limitations

### The Distinction That Matters

There is a critical legal distinction between:

### Competent Limited Representation:

> "I don't handle civil cases, but let me refer you to qualified counsel who does. You need to understand something critical: based on the constitutional violations we've documented in your criminal case—the First Amendment violations, the Fifth Amendment due process violations—you have federal civil rights claims under 42 U.S.C. § 1983. These carry a 3-year statute of limitations. Your 2015 SWAT incident: deadline is November 2018—two months from now. Your 2018 retaliation prosecution: deadline is May 2021. If you don't file before these deadlines, you will permanently and irreversibly lose the right to pursue them. You could lose millions of dollars.
> 
> 
> You need to retain civil counsel immediately—not next month, not after your criminal case resolves—right now. This is urgent. Do you understand?"
> 

### What Mr. Tipp Actually Said:

> "Give it time. These claims aren't well-supported. Filing a civil suit cloud appear retaliatory and potentially harm your criminal defense. You should find other counsel if you so choose."
> 

The difference is not scope of representation—it's whether the attorney provides adequate information to protect the client from foreseeable harm versus *actively misleading the client with legally false statements designed to deter constitutional litigation*.

### Why the "Scope of Representation" Argument Is Legally Incorrect

Even within a limited criminal defense scope, competent representation requires:

1. **Recognizing Related Legal Issues:** When an attorney documents constitutional violations in court filings (as Mr. Tipp did in his December 2019 Motion to Dismiss), competence requires recognizing that these same violations create federal civil rights claims.
2. **Protecting Client from Foreseeable Collateral Harm:** Criminal defense counsel must protect clients from foreseeable prejudice to related matters—including ensuring clients understand when civil deadlines are expiring.
3. **Avoiding Active Misinformation:** Competence requires attorneys to provide accurate legal information. Advising that civil litigation will "harm the defense" is legally false and constitutes active misinformation.

### Mischaracterization #2: "The Case Was Dismissed, So Representation Was Competent"

**ODC's Framing:** "Ultimately, the criminal case was dismissed, which suggests competent representation."

**Reality:** The outcome is not the sole measure of competence. An attorney who achieves dismissal by sacrificing a client's constitutional rights and causing $6+ million in collateral damage has not provided competent representation.

### The "How" Matters

- **Competent Dismissal:** Achieved by defending rights, exposing misconduct, and preserving related claims.
- **Incompetent Dismissal:** Achieved by trading rights, enabling institutional cover-ups, and allowing civil claims to expire.

Mr. Tipp chose the latter path.

## V. LEGAL ANALYSIS: MR. TIPP'S CONDUCT VIOLATED MULTIPLE RULES OF PROFESSIONAL CONDUCT

### Rule 1.1 (Competence) & Rule 1.3 (Diligence)

**Violation:** Failure to recognize and advise on expiring statutes of limitations for related civil claims constitutes a violation of both competence and diligence.

- **Competence:** A competent attorney in Mr. Tipp's position would recognize that documented constitutional violations give rise to civil claims with strict deadlines.
- **Diligence:** A diligent attorney would ensure the client understands the urgency of these deadlines, even if representation is limited to the criminal matter.

**Authority:** *In re Morin*, 408 N.W.2d 574 (Minn. 1987) (attorney suspended for failing to file a personal injury claim before the statute of limitations expired).

### Rule 1.4 (Communication)

**Violation:** Mr. Tipp failed to provide necessary information for me to make informed decisions. Specifically, he failed to explain:

- The irreversible nature of statute of limitations deadlines.
- The legal falsehood of his claim that civil litigation would "harm" my defense.
- The availability of referrals to competent civil rights counsel.

His advice to "give it time" was directly contrary to my interests.

### Rule 1.7 (Conflict of Interest)

**Violation:** Mr. Tipp's advice created a conflict between his interest in maintaining relationships with prosecutors and my interest in pursuing constitutional claims.

- By deterring civil litigation, he protected the YWCA and Missoula PD from accountability, thereby preserving his own professional standing at my expense.
- By trading my First Amendment rights, he prioritized an easy resolution over zealous advocacy.

### Rule 8.4(c) (Dishonesty, Fraud, Deceit, or Misrepresentation)

**Violation:** Mr. Tipp's statement that filing a civil suit would "appear retaliatory and harm your criminal defense" was a misrepresentation of law and fact.

- It is a legally incorrect statement designed to mislead.
- It constitutes deceit by omission by failing to disclose the expiring statutes of limitations.

**Authority:** Nebraska Ethics Advisory Opinion No. 13-04 (attorneys may not mislead clients about their legal options, even in areas outside the scope of representation).

### The Unacceptable Precedent of Trading Constitutional Rights

**ABA Resolution 113E (2018):** The American Bar Association has formally condemned the practice of prosecutors demanding that criminal defendants waive their rights to pursue civil action in exchange for a plea deal.

**Rationale:** Such waivers:

1. Undermine constitutional protections
2. Prevent accountability for institutional misconduct
3. Create improper attorney conflicts of interest
4. Transform rights into privileges

**Application to Tipp:** Mr. Tipp's negotiation of First Amendment waiver violates ABA standards adopted by Montana Rules of Professional Conduct.

### Deterring Constitutional Accountability as Ethical Violation

Courts have consistently held that deterring individuals from pursuing legal remedies violates public policy. In *Haddle v. Garrison*, 525 U.S. 121 (1998), the Supreme Court recognized that conspiracies to deter federal court participation are actionable civil rights violations. Similarly, *Jones v. Trump* held that agreements to suppress criminal prosecution violate public policy.

Mr. Tipp's advice that civil litigation would "harm my defense" served the same function: deterring constitutional accountability litigation to protect institutional actors. This violates the fundamental principle that attorneys may not obstruct clients' access to legal remedies.

### Comparative Analysis: Tipp vs. Montana Precedent

| Montana Precedent (7-Month Suspension) | Mr. Tipp's Conduct | Why Tipp's Is More Serious |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Missed single appeal deadline | Multiple statute failures ($6.4–8.4M) | Multiple claims, massive damages |
| Failed to communicate deadline | Active misinformation ("give it time" + "will harm defense") | Affirmative false advice, not mere omission |
| Failed to investigate case | Failed to investigate Brueckner conflict + 18-month delay | Admitted materiality, never investigated |
| Single client harmed | Constitutional precedent harming all future defendants | Systemic impact on rights |
| No conflict of interest | Trading constitutional rights + institutional loyalty | Multiple Rule 1.7 violations |

If Montana precedent warrants 7-month suspension for single deadline failures, Mr. Tipp's multiple deadline failures, active misinformation, constitutional rights trading, and $6+ million damages warrant at least equivalent—if not greater—sanction.

## VII. THE "DEFERRED PROSECUTION AGREEMENT" ISSUE: TRADING RIGHTS AS BARGAINING CHIPS

### Why This Violates Professional Conduct

The deferred prosecution agreement conditioned criminal dismissal on:

1. Waiver of First Amendment rights (removal of protected speech)
2. 12-month silence period (chilling future protected speech)
3. Admission to conduct Judge Warren ruled was protected
4. Ongoing monitoring and $50,000 bail (punishment for exercising rights)

This transforms constitutional rights into negotiable commodities—precisely what ethics rules prohibit.

### Legal Authority: Attorneys Cannot Negotiate Constitutional Rights Waivers

### Supreme Court — Perry v. Sindermann, 408 U.S. 593 (1972)

"The government cannot condition a benefit upon the waiver of constitutional rights. Even if a person has no 'right' to a valuable governmental benefit, the government may not deny a benefit on a basis that infringes his constitutionally protected interests—especially his interest in freedom of speech."

**Application:** Prosecutors cannot condition case dismissal on speech waivers; defense attorneys cannot negotiate such conditions.

### Nebraska Ethics Opinion & ABA Guidance

Both authorities hold that attorneys violate fiduciary duty by conditioning criminal resolution on constitutional rights waivers. Such agreements:

- Serve institutional interests over client interests
- Create conflicts of interest
- Undermine constitutional protections
- Violate public policy

### The "Rights vs. Privileges" Problem

ODC's dismissal ratifies the doctrine that constitutional rights become "privileges" when exercised under duress:

| Constitutional Framework | ODC-Approved Reality |
| --- | --- |
| Rights are inalienable | Rights can be traded for prosecutorial favor |
| Courts protect speech | Attorneys trade speech to expedite resolution |
| Rights apply equally | Rights apply only if you don't need them |
| Retaliation is prohibited | "Appearing retaliatory" justifies waiving rights |

If ODC's dismissal stands, Montana adopts the position that rights are conditional privileges, not fundamental protections.

## VIII. ODC'S ANALYTICAL ERRORS REQUIRING REVERSAL

### Error #1: Applying "Criminal Case Outcome" as Sole Competence Metric

**ODC's Reasoning:** "The criminal case was eventually dismissed, demonstrating competent representation."

**Why This Fails:** Competence is measured by *how* outcomes are achieved, not outcomes alone. An attorney who achieves dismissal by:

- Trading constitutional rights
- Causing $6+ million in collateral harm
- Delaying 18 months unnecessarily
- Refusing to investigate material conflicts

has NOT provided competent representation—even if the criminal case is eventually dismissed.

**Analogous Scenario:** An attorney who wins a criminal case by bribing jurors has achieved a favorable "outcome" but has committed serious misconduct. *How* matters.

### Error #2: Ignoring Active Misinformation vs. Mere Omission

**ODC's Finding:** Mr. Tipp told complainant to seek other counsel, demonstrating adequate communication.

**Why This Fails:** There is a critical distinction between:

1. **Passive scope limitation:** "I don't handle civil matters" = not sanctionable
2. **Active misinformation:** "Give it time; these claims aren't well-supported" = sanctionable
3. **Deliberate misrepresentation:** "Filing civil claims will harm your defense" = serious misconduct

Mr. Tipp's conduct falls into categories 2 and 3, not category 1.

**Evidence of Active Misinformation:**

- "Give it time" (June 30, 2025 email)
- "Claims aren't well-supported" (demonstrably false given documented constitutional violations)
- "Impractical from recovery standpoint" (ignoring § 1983 municipal liability and attorney fee provisions)
- **"Filing civil suit will appear retaliatory and harm your defense" (legally false statement designed to deter constitutional litigation)**

The most egregious example of Mr. Tipp's active misinformation was his explicit warning that filing civil rights claims would "appear retaliatory" and harm my criminal defense. This statement:

1. **Is legally false**—civil litigation is protected First Amendment activity that cannot be used against criminal defendants.
2. **Created false urgency**—by linking civil litigation to criminal jeopardy, Tipp manufactured a crisis requiring me to choose between rights.
3. **Evidences bad faith**—no competent attorney would believe civil rights litigation worsens criminal outcomes absent prosecutorial retaliation (itself illegal).

This is not passive omission—it is affirmative misrepresentation designed to deter constitutional accountability litigation.

### Error #3: Conflating "Informed Client" with "Client Who Sought Other Counsel"

**ODC's Finding:** Mr. Tipp adequately communicated because emails show I acknowledged seeking other counsel.

**Why This Fails:**

Seeking other counsel ≠ Understanding urgency

My emails referencing "other counsel" were in the context of:

- Mr. Tipp's advice to "give it time"
- His claims that civil action was "not well-supported"
- His failure to explain that statutes of limitations were about to expire

I was seeking counsel based on misinformation, not informed consent.

7. Material conflicts (like Detective Brueckner's YWCA board service) must be investigated

These findings protect future Montana defendants from similar misconduct.
