## Introduction

The documented evidence in **2016OPA-1167** reveals a systematic pattern of Fourth Amendment violations stemming from the deliberate falsification of police reports that transformed an initial finding of **no violation** into fabricated criminal charges. This case presents one of the most egregious examples of police report manipulation in the documented chain of custody from Edmonds Police Department to King County Sheriff's Department to Seattle Police Department, ultimately resulting in unlawful custodial detention based on **entirely fabricated evidence**.

## The Report Falsification Chain: A Constitutional Analysis

### Original Edmonds Police Determination

**Edmonds Police Officer Samuel Gagner (#2899)** conducted the initial investigation in report **2016-00009660** and made the following critical determinations[1]:

> "I confirmed that there is an active order of protection in place between Chard and Nuno, however, since Nuno did not attempt to contact Chard or have another party contact her on his behalf, there was no violation of the order"[1]
> 

Officer Gagner further documented Danielle Chard's mental state, noting that he "**provided her with a domestic resource pamphlet and took a statement from Chard for further evidence of Chard's erratic behavior for future court proceedings**"[1]. This establishes that the originating law enforcement officer found **no probable cause** for criminal charges and specifically documented concerns about the complainant's reliability.

### The Fabrication During Inter-Agency Transfer

As the report moved through the law enforcement chain—from Edmonds to King County Sheriff's Department to Seattle Police Department—the fundamental facts were **completely reversed**[1]. The final Seattle Police report **GO2016-148053** authored by **Officer Erik Eastgard (#8348)** now claimed[2]:

> "vChard showed me an email containing a PDF file of a police report. vChard advised the email is from sNuno. The email was addressed from elvis.nuno@gmail.com"[2]
> 

This transformation is constitutionally significant because:

1. **No email was ever produced or investigated**[3][4]
2. **No attempt was made to verify the existence of any email**[3][4]
3. **The alleged evidence contradicted the original finding of no violation**[1][5]

## Fourth Amendment Analysis: Fabricated Evidence and False Arrest

### Lack of Probable Cause Under *Beck v. Ohio*

Under **Beck v. Ohio**, 379 U.S. 89 (1964), probable cause requires "facts and circumstances within the officer's knowledge that are sufficient to warrant a prudent person, or one of reasonable caution, in believing, in the circumstances shown, that the suspect has committed, is committing, or is about to commit an offense"[6].

The evidence demonstrates a complete absence of probable cause:

1. **Original Investigation Found No Violation**: Officer Gagner's initial report explicitly stated no law was violated[1][5]
2. **No Supporting Evidence**: Despite claiming an email violation, no email was ever produced, investigated, or verified[3][4]
3. **Contradictory Timeline**: The Seattle report contradicted established facts from the originating jurisdiction[1][5]

### Fabricated Evidence Under *Brady v. Maryland*

The systematic alteration of police reports constitutes fabricated evidence under **Brady v. Maryland**, 373 U.S. 83 (1963)[7][8]. The prosecution's failure to disclose the discrepancies between the Edmonds and Seattle reports violated Brady obligations by withholding:

- Evidence that the original report found no violation[1][5]
- Documentation of the complainant's "erratic behavior"[1][5]
- The complete absence of any supporting evidence for the alleged email[3][4]

## Excessive Bail and Eighth Amendment Violations

### Constitutional Standards for Excessive Bail

Under **Stack v. Boyle**, 342 U.S. 1 (1951), bail is excessive when "set at a figure higher than an amount reasonably calculated to ensure the asserted governmental interest"[9][10]. The **$75,000 bail** (requiring $7,500 for bond) imposed in this case violated multiple constitutional principles:

1. **No Legitimate Governmental Interest**: With no actual evidence and the original report finding no violation, there was no legitimate interest served by any bail amount[11][12]
2. **Disproportionate to Alleged Offense**: The bail amount was grossly excessive for charges that **the original investigating officer determined were baseless**[1][5]
3. **Financial Incapacitation**: The amount effectively denied pretrial release, as documented in Nuno's statement that his family "struggled to gather $7,500 for bail" over **two weeks**[3][4]

### Impact on Presumption of Innocence

The excessive bail violated the principle established in *Stack* that "unless this right to bail before trial is preserved, the presumption of innocence...would lose its meaning"[9]. The **two-week detention** based on fabricated evidence effectively punished Nuno before any adjudication of guilt[3][4].

## Municipal Liability Under Section 1983

### *Monell* Doctrine Application

Under **Monell v. Department of Social Services**, 436 U.S. 658 (1978), municipalities may be held liable when constitutional violations result from official policy, custom, or deliberate indifference[13][14]. The evidence establishes multiple theories of municipal liability:

### 1. Custom of Report Fabrication

The systematic alteration of reports between agencies suggests an **unofficial custom** of modifying reports to support desired prosecutions rather than maintaining factual accuracy[13][15].

### 2. Failure to Train and Supervise

The complete failure of **supervisors and prosecutors** to compare the original Edmonds report with the Seattle version demonstrates deliberate indifference to constitutional requirements[11][12].

### 3. Ratification by Final Policymaker

**OPA Deputy Director Tonia Winchester's** dismissal of the complaint, despite documented evidence of report falsification, constitutes ratification by a final policymaker[3][4][5].

## OPA Response: Institutional Failure

### Winchester's Denial of Clear Evidence

Deputy Director Winchester's response demonstrates willful blindness to constitutional violations[3][4][5]:

> "SPD officers did not alter another agency's report, or influence the investigation conducted by another agency"[3][4][5]
> 

This statement directly contradicts the documented evidence showing:

- **Original report**: "no violation of the order"[1][5]
- **Seattle report**: "received an e-mail...in violation of the Order of Protection"[2]

### Brady Violation by OPA

OPA's failure to investigate the clear discrepancies between reports constitutes a Brady violation, as they had constructive knowledge of exculpatory evidence that was withheld from the defense[7][16][8].

## Civil Rights Violations Under 42 U.S.C. § 1983

### Individual Officer Liability

**Officer Erik Eastgard** and other involved officers are liable under § 1983 for[17][18][19]:

1. **Fabrication of Evidence**: Knowingly or recklessly creating false reports[17]
2. **False Arrest**: Arresting without probable cause[19][6]
3. **Malicious Prosecution**: Continuing prosecution despite knowledge of fabricated evidence[17]

### Loss of Qualified Immunity

Under established precedent, officers lose qualified immunity protection when they "knowingly filed a false affidavit" or acted "with reckless disregard for the truth"[17][20]. The documented discrepancies between reports clearly establish such recklessness.

## Conclusion

**2016OPA-1167** represents a textbook example of systematic Fourth Amendment violations through police report fabrication. The transformation of Officer Gagner's finding of "no violation" into Seattle PD's claim of email-based order violation, without any supporting evidence, constitutes one of the most clear-cut cases of evidence fabrication in recent legal history.

The **$75,000 bail** imposed based on this fabricated evidence violated the Eighth Amendment's prohibition on excessive bail, particularly given that the original investigating officer found no crime had occurred. The **two-week detention** resulting from the family's inability to immediately post $7,500 for bond compounds the constitutional injury.

OPA's dismissal of this complaint, despite overwhelming documentary evidence of report falsification, demonstrates institutional failure to address clear misconduct and may constitute a pattern and practice of constitutional violations sufficient to support municipal liability under *Monell*.

The case presents compelling claims under § 1983 for fabrication of evidence, false arrest, malicious prosecution, and municipal liability, with damages including unlawful detention, legal costs, reputational harm, and the broader constitutional injury of undermining faith in law enforcement integrity.
