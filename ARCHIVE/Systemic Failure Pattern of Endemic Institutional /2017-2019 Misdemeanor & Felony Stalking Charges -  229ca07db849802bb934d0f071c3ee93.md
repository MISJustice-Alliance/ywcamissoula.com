# II. Constitutional Violations During the Case

## A. Fourth Amendment Violations - Search and Seizure

### 1. **Overbroad Facebook Search Warrant**

The prosecution's filing of a search warrant with Facebook to obtain "*all* data contained in [Mr. Nuno's] account" represents a paradigmatic **Fourth Amendment violation** through an overbroad "fishing expedition." Under established precedent from cases like *ACLU v. D.C. Superior Court*, such warrants violate the Fourth Amendment's particularity requirement[1][2].

The search warrant violated multiple constitutional principles:

- **Lack of Particularity**: No specific scope or limitation on what evidence was sought[1]
- **Fishing Expedition**: Broad request for "all data" without nexus to alleged criminal activity[3]
- **Chilling Effect on Speech**: Targeting political and personal communications without justification[2]

Federal courts have consistently held that "the Fourth Amendment prohibits the government from performing broad fishing expeditions into private affairs"[3]. The warrant's failure to specify particular evidence sought makes it facially unconstitutional under the Supreme Court's holding in *Berger v. New York*.

### 2. **No Evidence Found Despite Invasive Search**

Significantly, the invasive Facebook search that violated Mr. Nuno's privacy rights found **no evidence of even mentioning E'Lise Chard**, confirming the baseless nature of the stalking allegations. This complete lack of evidence demonstrates both the improper motivation for the warrant and the absence of probable cause for the underlying charges.

## B. Eighth Amendment Violations - Excessive Bail

### 1. **$50,000-$100,000 Bail Demands for Non-Violent Speech Charges**

The prosecution's demands for bail amounts between $50,000-$100,000 for non-violent, speech-related charges constitute clear **Eighth Amendment violations**[Previous Analysis]. Montana Constitution Article II, Section 22 prohibits "excessive bail," adopting the same language as the Eighth Amendment[4].

Under the Supreme Court's framework in *Salerno*, pretrial detention must be supported by "clear and convincing evidence" and serve narrow governmental interests[5][6]. The excessive bail demands here served no legitimate purpose given:

- No history of violence or flight risk
- Charges based entirely on alleged electronic communications
- Complete absence of evidence supporting the allegations
- Mr. Nuno's compliance with all previous release conditions

### 2. **Constitutional Standards for Bail Setting**

Recent scholarship confirms that "the government is constitutionally obligated to prove the basis for pretrial detention by clear and convincing evidence"[5]. The prosecution's inability to provide such evidence while demanding excessive bail amounts violated both federal and Montana constitutional protections against excessive bail[7][8].

# III. Electronic Monitoring and Civil Rights Violations

## A. 18-Month Ankle Monitor Restriction

Mr. Nuno was subjected to electronic ankle monitoring for over a year, restricting his movements outside Missoula County and within 1,000 yards of the YWCA offices. This restriction constituted both **false imprisonment** and **Fourth Amendment violations** under recent precedent.

### 1. **Fourth Amendment Analysis of Electronic Monitoring**

In *Grady v. North Carolina*, the Supreme Court held that "attaching a GPS tracking device to a person—even a convicted sex offender—constitutes a Fourth Amendment search and is thus subject to the inquiry of reasonableness"[13]. For pretrial defendants, electronic monitoring restrictions must be justified by compelling governmental interests and narrowly tailored to serve those interests.

The ACLU has documented that "electronic monitoring is incarceration in another form: e-carceration"[14]. Research shows that EM "does not achieve its purported outcomes of improving public safety, aiding in rehabilitation and ensuring court attendance"[14].

### 2. **Technological and Privacy Violations**

Electronic monitoring devices often have capabilities for "eavesdropping and recording conversations," creating additional constitutional violations[15]. Many defendants are unaware that their devices can monitor conversations, including privileged attorney-client communications, violating:

- **Fourth Amendment privacy rights**
- **Federal Wiretap Act** provisions
- **Attorney-client privilege** protections[15]

## B. Geographic Restrictions as Punishment

The 1,000-yard restriction from YWCA offices effectively prevented Mr. Nuno from accessing significant portions of downtown Missoula, interfering with his ability to:

- Conduct business operations
- Access legal services
- Maintain professional relationships
- Exercise basic liberty interests

This geographic restriction served no legitimate safety purpose, as no evidence existed of any contact or threat toward E'Lise Chard or YWCA facilities.

## A. Pattern of Malicious Prosecution

The prosecution's conduct demonstrates a clear pattern of **malicious prosecution** and **abuse of process**:

### 1. **Filing Charges Without Probable Cause**

Montana stalking statute MCA § 45-5-220 requires a "course of conduct" that would cause a "reasonable person" to "fear for the person's own safety" or "suffer substantial emotional distress"[16][17]. The evidence clearly failed to support these elements:

- **No Course of Conduct**: Only three brief emails requesting cessation of harassment[Previous Analysis]
- **No Fear Induced**: E'Lise Chard's own testimony acknowledged Mr. Nuno posed no threat
- **No Substantial Contact**: Parties were essentially strangers with minimal interaction history
- **Constitutional Protection**: The emails constituted protected petition activity

### 2. **Coordination with Private Parties**

The prosecution's coordination with E'Lise Chard and YWCA employees to manufacture criminal charges violates ethical obligations and constitutional protections. Officer Ethan Smith's documented "close personal relationship" with E'Lise Chard created an obvious conflict of interest that should have disqualified him from the investigation[Previous Analysis].

## B. 18-Month Delay in Obvious Case

The prosecution's prolonging of obviously meritless charges for 18 months constitutes **abuse of process** and **deliberate indifference** to constitutional rights. Basic investigation would have revealed:

1. **No Criminal Relationship**: Parties had no significant personal history
2. **No Evidence of Stalking**: Three emails requesting cessation of harassment do not constitute stalking
3. **Protected Speech**: The communications fell within First Amendment protections
4. **Fabricated Allegations**: E'Lise Chard's statements were demonstrably false
