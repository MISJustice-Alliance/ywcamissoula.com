## I. Fourth Amendment Violations in the Facebook Search Warrant

### A. Constitutional Requirements for Digital Search Warrants

The Fourth Amendment requires search warrants to "particularly describe... the persons or things to be seized"[2][3]. This particularity requirement serves to "minimize officers' intrusions upon citizens' privacy" and prevents "general, exploratory rummaging in a person's belongings"[2]. As the Supreme Court explained in *Marron v. United States*, the requirement "makes general searches... impossible and prevents the seizure of one thing under a warrant describing another"[2][3].

For digital evidence, courts have increasingly recognized that the particularity requirement "assumes even greater importance" because technological advances "have rendered the computer hard drive akin to a residence in terms of the scope and quantity of private information it may contain"[4]. Social media accounts like Facebook contain vast amounts of personal information including:

- Private messages and communications
- Political affiliations and "liked" organizations
- Reading habits through shared articles
- Personal views on political, economic, religious, and social issues
- Location data and check-ins
- Photographic evidence of personal relationships[5]

### B. The Facebook Warrant as an Unconstitutional Fishing Expedition

The prosecution's warrant seeking "*all* data contained in [Mr. Nuno's] account" violated multiple constitutional principles:

### 1. **Lack of Particularity**

The warrant failed to specify what evidence was sought or how it related to the alleged stalking charges. Research confirms that "a demand for 'all contents of communications' related to a Facebook account is therefore a classically overbroad fishing expedition, rather than a particularized search"[5]. The warrant could have been properly limited by:

- Certain keywords related to the alleged crimes
- Communications with particular individuals (E'Lise Chard)
- Specific time periods related to the alleged conduct
- Particular types of evidence relevant to stalking charges[5]

### 2. **General Warrant Characteristics**

The ACLU has documented that such warrants constitute illegal "general warrants" because they provide "a vague description of the evidence to be seized, allowing officers to carry out an almost unrestricted search"[1][6]. Federal courts consistently hold that "the Fourth Amendment prohibits the government from performing broad fishing expeditions into private affairs"[1].

### 3. **Chilling Effect on Protected Speech**

The overbroad warrant created an impermissible chilling effect on Mr. Nuno's First Amendment rights by subjecting all his political speech, associations, and personal communications to government scrutiny without nexus to criminal activity[1][5].

### C. Complete Absence of Evidence

Significantly, the invasive Facebook search that violated Mr. Nuno's privacy rights found **no evidence of even mentioning E'Lise Chard**, confirming both the absence of probable cause and the improper fishing expedition nature of the warrant. This complete lack of relevant evidence demonstrates that:

- The warrant was not supported by probable cause
- The broad scope was unnecessary and unconstitutional
- The prosecution proceeded with charges despite knowing no evidence existed
- The search violated Fourth Amendment protections without any law enforcement benefit

## IV. Legal Precedent for Facebook Search Warrant Violations

### A. Established Constitutional Violations

Courts have consistently held that overbroad social media warrants violate the Fourth Amendment:

### 1. **Facebook, Inc. v. New York County DA (2017)**

Facebook challenged 381 search warrants demanding comprehensive account information, arguing they were "overbroad and lacked particularity"[9][10][11]. While the court ruled on procedural grounds, it acknowledged concerns about bulk warrants and their constitutional implications.

### 2. **ACLU v. Whatcom County Sheriff (2017)**

The ACLU successfully challenged a Facebook warrant as an "unlawful intrusion on the First Amendment right of free speech" and a violation of Fourth Amendment particularity requirements[6]. The sheriff withdrew the warrant rather than defend its constitutionality.

### 3. **Larry Young v. State (2023)**

The Florida 4th District Court of Appeals clearly established that "Facebook messages are protected by the 4th Amendment" and require proper constitutional procedures[12].
