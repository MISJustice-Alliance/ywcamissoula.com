## Case Summary

This analysis evaluates the specific allegations made in Mr Nuno’s 2016 Washington State Bar Association (WSBA) complaint against attorney Patricia Fulton regarding her representation in the Edmonds Municipal Court matter (DV Assault 3rd Degree).

While the WSBA closed this complaint without public discipline, a review of the conduct described against Washington's Rules of Professional Conduct (RPC) indicates multiple likely violations of ethical standards. Specifically, Ms. Fulton’s advice to default on a protection order, failure to conduct basic discovery (obtaining police reports), and refusal to interview exculpatory witnesses constitute a breach of the duties of **Competence (RPC 1.1)**, **Diligence (RPC 1.3)**, and **Communication (RPC 1.4)**.

The Bar's dismissal likely resulted from the high evidentiary standard required for disciplinary action ("clear and preponderance of the evidence") versus the lower standard for civil malpractice ("preponderance of the evidence"). However, the facts documented here establish a clear pattern of *Ineffective Assistance of Counsel* that likely prejudiced the outcome of Mr Nuno’s case, forcing a plea deal based on incomplete information.

# 2. Detailed Ethical Analysis (Washington RPCs)

### A. Misconduct Regarding Protection Order (RPC 1.1, 1.2, 1.4)

**Allegation:** Ms. Fulton advised Mr Nuno *not* to attend the hearing for the Order of Protection, claiming it was futile, and later admitted this default prevented any future appeal—directly contradicting Mr Nuno’s stated goal to contest the false accusations.

- **RPC 1.2 (Scope of Representation):** A lawyer must abide by a client's decisions concerning the *objectives* of representation. Mr Nuno’s objective was explicitly to contest the false accusations in the Order to preserve his record. By advising a course of action (non-attendance) that permanently waived Mr Nuno’s right to contest these findings, Ms. Fulton arguably usurped his authority over the objectives of the case.
- **RPC 1.4 (Communication):** Lawyers must explain a matter "to the extent reasonably necessary to permit the client to make informed decisions." Failing to warn Mr Nuno that missing the hearing would result in a permanent, non-appealable finding of fact was a critical failure of communication. Had Mr Nuno known the default was irreversible, his decision would likely have been different.

### B. Failure to Investigate & Diligence (RPC 1.1, 1.3)

**Allegation:** Ms. Fulton refused to interview key witnesses (therapist, Ms. Chard’s family member) and failed to obtain vital discovery (police reports from the related Seattle case) before advising Mr Nuno to plead guilty.

- **RPC 1.3 (Diligence):** A lawyer must act with "reasonable diligence and promptness." In criminal defense, this includes a duty to conduct a reasonable factual investigation.
- **Legal Standard (Strickland v. Washington):** Courts have held that counsel’s failure to investigate key witnesses or obtain readily available police reports constitutes "deficient performance."
    - **Witnesses:** The refusal to interview witnesses who could attest to the accuser’s mental health history or Mr Nuno’s lack of dangerousness deprived him of a potential defense strategy (e.g., attacking the accuser's credibility or intent).
    - **Discovery:** Failing to obtain the Seattle police reports—which Mr Nuno later found contained exculpatory evidence or proof of perjury—meant she advised him to accept a plea deal without understanding the full strength (or weakness) of the state's case. This is a "failure to investigate" that often grounds successful Ineffective Assistance of Counsel claims.

### C. Conflict of Interest (RPC 1.7)

**Allegation:** Ms. Fulton claimed a "close personal relationship" with the prosecutor (former student/professor dynamic) would be advantageous.

- **RPC 1.7 (Conflict of Interest: Current Clients):** A conflict exists if there is a significant risk that the representation will be "materially limited" by the lawyer's personal interest or relationship with a third party.
- **Analysis:** While knowing a prosecutor is not inherently a violation, if Ms. Fulton pulled punches or encouraged a plea to maintain her amicable relationship with the prosecutor rather than zealously advocating for Mr Nuno, this constitutes a "material limitation" conflict. Her dismissiveness of Mr Nuno’s innocence claims typically signals a lawyer who has prejudged the case to preserve professional relationships over client interests.

### D. Mental Health Evaluation Misconduct

**Allegation:** Ms. Fulton "poisoned the well" with Associated Behavioral Health Center (ABHC), leading them to refuse an evaluation or refund.

- **RPC 1.6 (Confidentiality):** Lawyers generally cannot reveal information relating to representation without client consent. If Ms. Fulton made disparaging remarks to ABHC about Mr Nuno’s mental state *before* the evaluation, she may have violated her duty of loyalty and confidentiality, actively sabotaging the defense evidence she was supposed to be generating.

# 3. Why the Bar Complaint Was Likely Closed

This is common for several systemic reasons, even when misconduct occurred:

1. **Standard of Proof:** Disciplinary counsel must prove violations by a "clear preponderance" of the evidence. In "he-said/she-said" disputes regarding verbal advice (e.g., "She told me not to go" vs. "I advised him of the risks"), the Bar often declines to prosecute due to lack of written proof.
2. **"Strategic Decision" Defense:** Bars are reluctant to second-guess an attorney's strategic choices. Ms. Fulton likely responded that advising you to skip the hearing was a "strategy" to avoid self-incrimination while criminal charges were pending. While this strategy was disastrous for your long-term civil rights, the Bar often views it as a "judgment call" rather than an ethical breach.
3. **Lack of "Clear" Harm (at the time):** In 2016, the full extent of the damage (the 10-year civil rights saga) had not yet materialized. The Bar may have viewed it as a standard plea negotiation dispute.

# 4. Strategic Relevance to Current Civil Rights Litigation

Although the specific malpractice claim against Ms. Fulton is time-barred (3-year statute of limitations in WA expired ~2019), this 2016 Bar Complaint is a critical evidentiary document for the broader Civil Rights case:

- **Pattern of Systemic Failure:** It documents that a lack of defense was not due to Mr Nuno’s guilt, but due to *institutional failure* starting at the very beginning. This rebuts any argument that Mr Nuno’s 2016 conviction validates the later harassment.
- **"But-For" Causation:** but for Fulton's initial negligence (waiving the protection order hearing), the subsequent "stalking" charges (often based on violating such orders) would have had no legal basis.
- **Proof of Diligence:** The fact that Mr Nuno filed this detailed complaint in 2016 proves he was actively trying to alert the system to the corruption/malpractice contemporaneously, counteracting any "laches" (unreasonable delay) arguments defendants might raise now.
