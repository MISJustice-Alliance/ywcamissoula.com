# I. Constitutional Violations Under Federal Law

## A. Fourteenth Amendment Due Process Violations

The police threats against Mr. Nuno's romantic partner constitute clear violations of substantive due process under the Fourteenth Amendment. Under established precedent in *Brady v. Maryland* and *Walker v. Johnston*, external pressures that fundamentally undermine plea voluntariness violate constitutional protections[1].

### 1. **Coercive Plea Bargaining Framework**

Research confirms that "coercive plea bargaining has poisoned the criminal justice system"[2] and creates "damned if you do, damned if you don't" scenarios that violate constitutional protections. The Supreme Court has held that "agents of the State may not produce a plea by actual or threatened physical harm or by mental coercion overbearing the will of the defendant"[3].

The 2020 threats meet all elements of unconstitutional coercion:

- **State Actor Involvement**: Police officers making explicit threats
- **Coercive Tactics**: Threat of family destruction through child removal
- **Third-Party Pressure**: Targeting vulnerable witness to influence defendant
- **Impossible Choice**: No constitutional path to fair resolution

### 2. **The Impossible Catch-22 Situation**

The witness intimidation created a paradigmatic "catch-22" situation where Mr. Nuno faced impossible choices:

| **Option** | **Action** | **Consequence** | **Constitutional Violation** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **A** | Acknowledge coercion in court | Immediate retaliation against witness | Due process violation through retaliation |
| **B** | Remain silent about threats | Allow tainted plea process to proceed | Due process violation through coerced plea |

This situation violates the Supreme Court's holding in *United States v. Jackson* that the state cannot "impose an impermissible burden upon the exercise of a constitutional right"[4]. The coercive structure prevented Mr. Nuno from exercising his constitutional rights without triggering immediate harm to an innocent third party.

## B. Fifth Amendment Self-Incrimination Violations

The coerced pleas violated Mr. Nuno's Fifth Amendment protection against compelled self-incrimination. Under *Boykin v. Alabama*, guilty pleas must be "voluntary and intelligent"[5]. The witness intimidation rendered the pleas involuntary by creating external coercion that "overbore the will of the defendant"[3].

### 1. **Involuntary Waiver of Trial Rights**

By entering guilty pleas to protect his romantic partner from child custody threats, Mr. Nuno was forced to waive fundamental constitutional rights:

# III. Legal Precedent for Constitutional Violations

## A. Witness Intimidation Under Federal Law

Federal law explicitly criminalizes witness intimidation under 18 U.S.C. § 1512, which prohibits "knowingly us[ing] intimidation, threaten[ing], or corruptly persuad[ing] another person"[8]. The police threats to remove custody from Mr. Nuno's romantic partner meet all elements of this federal crime:

1. **Intimidation**: Explicit threats regarding child custody
2. **Purpose**: To influence testimony against Mr. Nuno
3. **State Actor**: Police officer acting under color of law
4. **Interstate Impact**: Affecting Washington cases while coordinated with Montana harassment

## B. Coercive Plea Bargaining Jurisprudence

Courts have increasingly recognized that "coercive plea bargaining undermines the constitutional rights of defendants"[9] and violates fundamental fairness principles. The scholarly consensus confirms that plea bargaining creates "unconstitutional conditions" when defendants face excessive pressure[4].

### 1. **Brady Material and Plea Negotiations**

Recent scholarship argues that "due process requires disclosure of Brady material at the plea-bargaining phase"[1] because withholding exculpatory evidence leads defendants to "overestimate the likelihood of conviction and therefore to enter guilty pleas that do not reflect an accurate assessment"[1].

The witness intimidation constitutes a form of Brady violation by concealing the coercive tactics used to manufacture testimony, preventing Mr. Nuno from making an informed decision about plea negotiations.

### 2. **Prosecutorial Vindictiveness**

The coordination between prosecutors and law enforcement to intimidate witnesses represents prosecutorial vindictiveness under *Bordenkircher v. Hayes*[10]. While prosecutors have discretion in charging decisions, they cannot use coercive tactics that violate constitutional protections.
