# Systemic Civil Rights Violations, Institutional Misconduct, and Victim Abuse: Expanded History

### Introduction & Case Summary

The civil rights violations documented in the case of Elvis Ryland Nuno represent not an isolated incident but rather a manifestation of deeply entrenched institutional corruption within Missoula, Montana's law enforcement, prosecutorial, and victim advocacy systems. This report expands the evidentiary record to encompass systematic patterns of abuse affecting multiple victims across more than a decade, revealing coordinated misconduct by the YWCA Missoula, Missoula Police Department, and Missoula County Attorney's Office that preceded, paralleled, and followed Mr. Nuno's experience.

The documented evidence establishes that Mr. Nuno's case—characterized by Detective Connie Brueckner's unprecedented dual role as both YWCA Board member and investigating officer, retaliatory prosecution following protected First Amendment complaints, warrantless home invasions, and career destruction—fits within a broader pattern of institutional capture that has victimized multiple populations including sexual assault survivors, domestic violence victims, YWCA clients, individuals with mental illness, disabled persons, and families seeking services.

### I. The 2012–2014 Federal Crisis: Missoula as "America's Rape Capital"

#### A. The University of Montana Football Scandal

Between 2010 and 2014, Missoula, Montana—a city of 86,000 whose economy and identity centered on University of Montana "Griz Nation" football—became the epicenter of a national sexual assault scandal revealing catastrophic institutional failures across law enforcement, prosecutorial, and university systems.

**Scale of the Crisis:**

* 350 sexual assault cases reported to Missoula police (January 2008–May 2012)
* 80 reports of sexual assault in Missoula over three years, including 11 on campus
* 9 alleged rapes/sexual assaults involving students (September 2010–December 2011)
* At least 3 cases involved University of Montana football players
* Only 14 of 85 police referrals prosecuted by County Attorney (16.5% prosecution rate)

The scandal gained national attention through high-profile cases involving Grizzlies football players, ultimately resulting in federal investigations, the firing of the football coach and athletic director, and what Jezebel magazine dubbed Missoula "America's Rape Capital".

#### B. Documented Football-Related Sexual Assault Cases

**Beau Donaldson (Running Back)**

On New Year's Day 2012, 260-pound running back Beau Donaldson raped his close childhood friend Allison Huguet while she slept in his apartment. Donaldson confessed to police and to Huguet in a recorded phone call. Despite this confession, the case exemplified systemic prosecution failures. Donaldson ultimately pleaded guilty in September 2012 and received a 30-year sentence (20 suspended) in January 2013—but only after victim Huguet overcame profound barriers.

Huguet described the institutional pressure: "I didn't think I was strong enough... I knew going forward would be huge ordeal, and him playing for the Griz maximized it by a hundred". She initially declined to report because "I started acting crazy...I wanted to pay people to hurt him. I wanted to slash his tires...that's not my normal character".

**Jordan Johnson (Quarterback)**

On February 4, 2012, star quarterback Jordan Johnson allegedly raped an acquaintance in her home while watching a movie. The victim testified Johnson held her down despite repeated protests of "no, not tonight," with Johnson allegedly responding "I will make you". Immediately after, she texted her roommate: "OMG...I think I might have just gotten raped...he kept pushing and pushing and I said no but he wouldn't listen".

Johnson was charged in July 2012, suspended from the team, and faced up to 100 years imprisonment. However, in February 2013, a jury acquitted him after deliberating two hours. Johnson later received a $245,000 settlement from the State of Montana. The defense successfully argued the victim became upset only because Johnson "didn't talk to her other than to say 'Well, thanks'" after sex.

The timing of institutional response revealed priorities: Football coach Robin Pflugrad publicly praised Johnson's "character and tremendous moral fiber" three days before University President Royce Engstrom fired both Pflugrad and Athletic Director Jim O'Day in April 2012.

**Kerry Barrett (Gang Rape—No Charges)**

Kerry Barrett alleged gang rape by four University of Montana football players after consuming 8-11 shots of 99-proof alcohol within one hour at a party. She described being "in and out...penis in my face" and pushing the first player away before he forced himself on her, followed by three additional players.

Detectives determined there was "not probable cause to file criminal charges." The rationale: Barrett told police the men "would have likely believed it was consensual sex" and that she was too intoxicated to resist. No criminal charges were filed. The university expelled one alleged perpetrator, secured voluntary departure from another, but two had already left before action.

Barrett's statement captures the impact: "I just loved the atmosphere of the big stadium, all the people cheering. I loved it. And it is now ruined for me".

#### C. U.S. Department of Justice Investigation and Findings

On May 1, 2012, U.S. Attorney General Eric Holder announced DOJ investigations stating: "The allegations that the University of Montana, the local police, and the County Attorney's Office failed to adequately address sexual assault are very disturbing". Three simultaneous federal investigations commenced:

1. **U.S. Department of Justice**: Investigating Missoula Police Department, County Attorney's Office, and University of Montana Office of Public Safety
2. **U.S. Department of Education**: Campus sexual harassment and assault
3. **NCAA**: Athletic program conduct (ongoing since January 2012)

**DOJ Findings on Missoula County Attorney's Office (February 2014):**

The DOJ found "substantial evidence" that the Missoula County Attorney's Office, led since 1998 by Fred Van Valkenburg, engaged in systematic gender discrimination fueled by gender bias:

* **"General disregard"** for sexual assault cases involving female victims
* **Failed to prosecute** cases even with confessions or eyewitnesses
* **Declined prosecution** of "nearly every case where assault was facilitated by drugs/alcohol...even when assailant had confessed"
* **Gang rape case declined** despite being "most egregious," citing "lack of evidence"
* **No training** provided to Deputy County Attorneys on sexual assault investigation or prosecution
* **Revictimization**: Victims described interactions as "traumatic," feeling "no compassion," "judged," that prosecutors "didn't believe them"

**Specific Prosecutorial Misconduct:**

* Deputy County Attorney quoted **religious passages** to sexual assault victim, which victim interpreted as judgment "for having made the report"
* Prosecutor told mother of 5-year-old sexual assault victim: **"boys will be boys"**
* Deputy County Attorney told victim: **"all you want is revenge"**
* One victim stated she **"would never suggest"** another woman pursue prosecution in Missoula

**County Attorney Response:**

Rather than cooperating, County Attorney Van Valkenburg "strongly opposed" the investigation, "denied any mishandling," and in February 2014 filed a federal lawsuit against the DOJ claiming overreach. Van Valkenburg's office "refused to provide requested documents, information, or access to staff for interviews".

The DOJ was forced to gather information through alternative sources. A settlement was ultimately reached June 10, 2014, requiring comprehensive reforms. Montana's Attorney General certified full compliance on August 16, 2016, after implementation of a Special Victims Unit, victim-witness coordinator, trauma counselor, and revised policies.

#### D. Institutional Culture Enabling Abuse

Former University of Montana journalism professor John C. Williams characterized the institutional environment: "The football team was recruiting too many thugs. Rape was not the only problem. There was vandalism, personal assaults, destruction of property. Players were pampered and adored. Too many had the feeling they were bulletproof and immune to rules. They acted like arrogant marauders".

This federal investigation and settlement established critical context: the same Missoula law enforcement and prosecutorial entities that systematically discriminated against sexual assault victims through gender bias created the institutional environment in which subsequent abuses—including those documented in Mr. Nuno's case—would flourish. The YWCA Missoula, explicitly identified in the 2014 DOJ settlement as a "community partner" in reformed processes, subsequently became a vehicle for institutional retaliation rather than victim advocacy.

### II. YWCA Missoula: From Victim Advocacy to Institutional Weapon

#### A. Structural Corruption: Detective Connie Brueckner's Dual Role

The most egregious institutional conflict enabling systematic abuse is Detective Connie Brueckner's simultaneous service on the YWCA Missoula Board of Directors while investigating complaints against YWCA employees. ProPublica nonprofit records conclusively establish Brueckner as a YWCA Board member-at-large with her employer identified as "Missoula PD".

This arrangement creates:

1. **Institutional Capture**: Law enforcement serving organizational interests rather than public safety
2. **Fiduciary Conflict**: Board duties to protect YWCA conflicting with investigative objectivity
3. **Constitutional Violation**: Denial of due process through biased investigation
4. **Chilling Effect**: Survivors whose abusers are officers cannot safely seek services

**Empirical Context:**

Law enforcement officers perpetrate domestic violence at 2-4 times the rate of the general population, with best estimates around 21% (ranging 7-29% in U.S. studies). Earlier research documented rates up to 40% under broader definitions. Departmental accountability failures include light discipline, rare prosecution, and documented cases of officers with sustained DV findings advancing in rank.

Within this empirical reality, seating an MPD detective on the board of a domestic violence shelter creates a **non-waivable structural conflict**. The organization must be capable of adversarial advocacy against police actors, including officer-abusers, yet has seated police leadership in its oversight body. Recusal cannot cure the survivor-perceived risk or institutional capture.

### III. Missoula Police Department: Pattern of Excessive Force and Zero Accountability

#### A. Police Accountability Metrics (2016-2022)

Police Scorecard data for Missoula Police Department reveals systematic failure to hold officers accountable:

* **349 total civilian complaints** filed (2016-2022)
* **Only 16% ruled in favor of civilians**
* **4 use-of-force complaints**: **0% ruled in favor of civilians**
* **6 discrimination complaints**: **0% ruled in favor of civilians**
* **Alleged crimes by police**: Data not available

This near-zero accountability rate for use-of-force and discrimination complaints creates an environment of impunity enabling continued constitutional violations.

Furthermore, [as reported by The Pulp](https://thepulp.org/montana-blocks-access-to-basic-police-data/), Montana POST reversed prior practice. In 2017 and 2019, POST released officer rosters to reporters. In 2022, it refused an Associated Press request for names and employers.

That about-face tracked POST’s loss of independence. In 2019, lawmakers reorganized POST under the Department of Justice. In 2021, AG Austin Knudsen’s staff began supervising records responses. Council members raised separation-of-powers concerns. They described DOJ directing a quasi-judicial body’s disclosures.

Knudsen and DOJ lawyers argued statewide officer lists create privacy risks. They cited Montana’s constitutional right to privacy. They warned names could enable doxxing and harassment. They also raised cartel-threat scenarios and litigation fears. In May 2022, the council voted after a closed session. It limited disclosure to officers sanctioned by POST.

POST later turned that position into formal policy. It stopped releasing database exports of officer identity and job history. It now responds only to officer-by-officer lookups. This blocks tracking “wandering officers” across departments. It also blocks research on lateral transfers into Montana. That undermines statewide accountability and public trust. This makes Montana an outlier among states sharing this data. This significantly reduces transparency and public oversight.

#### B. James Dimitri Owens: Excessive Force Against Individual with Mental Illness (June 30, 2025)

Owens's mother, Connie Schade, described approximately 18 months of psychiatric crisis leading to homelessness, multiple short-term hospitalizations, and repeated police encounters. While praising some previous crisis team interactions, Schade argued the June 30 response exemplified a **"broken system"** where **"lack of specialized training for law enforcement leads to more struggles for people in acute crisis"**.

The Missoula County Sheriff's Office confirmed a "use of force incident" occurred and stated "a standard review is underway"—the same review process that resulted in zero use-of-force complaints being ruled in favor of civilians from 2016-2022.
