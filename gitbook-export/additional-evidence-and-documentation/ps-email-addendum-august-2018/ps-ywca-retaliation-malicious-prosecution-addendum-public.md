# Public Addendum: P.S. Email and Malicious Prosecution Concerns

## Overview

This public addendum explains why the August 14, 2018 P.S. email and forwarding chain matter to the public record concerning the criminal case against Elvis Nuno and the broader concerns about how complaints were handled once they reached YWCA and law-enforcement channels. The narrow public point is that a third-party message favorable to Elvis Nuno and critical of E'Lise Chard appears in discovery as having been redirected into police and victim-services channels rather than appearing, on the current record, to trigger neutral review or visible corrective action.

This version is revised for publication on a public evidentiary site and keeps the sender anonymized as **P.S.**. It is intended to document an evidentiary concern relevant to public understanding of the case record, not to make a final legal finding on malicious prosecution.

## What the Exhibit Shows

The discovery record shows that P.S. emailed YWCA's general inbox on August 14, 2018 under the subject line "Attn: Regarding conduct of employee E'Lise Chard." The email described Elvis Nuno as kind, respectful, generous, and non-abusive, stated that E'Lise Chard had been causing harassment and trouble for Elvis and his family, and asked YWCA to address what was "really going on."

The same record shows that nineteen minutes later E'Lise Chard forwarded that message to Detective Arianna Adams and Kim Harvey, stating: "Here is an email to the YWCA, about me, on Elvis' behalf. Not sure if you want this, since it's purportedly not from Elvis, but I figured I'd send it anyway." Kim Harvey later forwarded the chain again to Arianna Adams on August 23, 2018, preserving the message inside a law-enforcement or victim-services record stream.

## Why It Matters to the Public Record

For a public audience, the significance of this document is not that it proves every element of a malicious-prosecution claim by itself. Its significance is that it shows contradictory or favorable information entering the same institutional environment in which the criminal case continued.

The email was favorable to Elvis Nuno in at least two ways visible on its face: it presented supportive character information and it challenged E'Lise Chard's conduct and credibility. Yet the available record shows that this information was carried into investigative channels by E'Lise herself, not clearly processed as a complaint against her.

## Relationship to the Larger Case Pattern

Existing chronology materials describe a broader sequence in which complaints to YWCA were followed by police escalation, involvement of Detective Connie Brueckner while she was also tied to YWCA governance, prolonged criminal proceedings, and eventual dismissal with prejudice. Those same materials describe the prosecution as continuing despite weak or disputed factual support and despite concerns about institutional conflict.

Within that larger sequence, the P.S. email matters because it appears to add one more piece of information that cut against a one-sided prosecution narrative. For public readers, that makes the document relevant to the question whether the criminal process was moving forward in spite of accumulating warning signs rather than because of a neutral and balanced assessment of all available information.

## Public Questions Raised

This exhibit supports several narrow questions that are appropriate for a public evidentiary page.

- Did anyone in the investigative or prosecutorial chain treat this email as information requiring reassessment of witness credibility or case theory?
- Was the message treated as favorable or contradictory information, or only as something potentially suspicious because E'Lise framed it as being "on Elvis' behalf"?
- Why does the available record show supportive third-party communication being routed into police and victim-services channels rather than clearly into an internal complaint process?
- If the email did not lead to any separate charge, what does that suggest about how authorities themselves viewed its actual evidentiary value?

These are public-accountability questions about process, credibility handling, and escalation. They do not require the site to prove a legal claim in order to show why the document matters.

## Public Framing of Malicious Prosecution Concerns

For publication purposes, the strongest and safest framing is restrained: the P.S. email appears to show that information favorable to Elvis Nuno and adverse to E'Lise Chard was present in the broader case ecosystem while the criminal matter continued. That is relevant to public concerns about whether contradictory information was being absorbed, ignored, or reframed rather than prompting caution.

The forwarding language is part of why the exhibit is notable. E'Lise characterized the message as being "on Elvis' behalf" while acknowledging it was only "purportedly" so, which can reasonably be read as reframing an independent third-party complaint into something potentially attributable to Elvis himself. For public readers, that detail helps explain why the document may bear on questions of bias, narrative control, and the way supportive information was processed once it entered the case stream.

## What This Addendum Does Not Claim

This public addendum does not claim that the P.S. email alone proves malice, proves lack of probable cause on every charge, or establishes liability against every person mentioned in the broader record. It also does not try to resolve legal questions that depend on full official files, testimony, and attorney review.

The narrower conclusion supported here is that the email is a documented example of favorable and contradictory information entering the same institutional channels through which the case continued, and that this is relevant to public evaluation of whether the process reflected neutral case review or something more adversarial and self-reinforcing.
