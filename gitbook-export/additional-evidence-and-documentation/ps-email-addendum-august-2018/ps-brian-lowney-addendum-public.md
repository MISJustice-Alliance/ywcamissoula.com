# Public Addendum: P.S. Email and Questions About the Brian Lowney Prosecution Record

## Overview

This public addendum explains why the August 14, 2018 P.S. email and its forwarding chain matter to the public record concerning the Missoula prosecution and the role of prosecutor Brian Lowney within that broader case environment. The narrow public-interest point is that a third-party message favorable to Elvis Nuno and critical of E'Lise Chard appears to have entered prosecution-adjacent channels while the criminal matter continued.

This version is revised for publication on a public evidentiary site and keeps the sender anonymized as **P.S.**. It is intended to identify a document-based accountability question, not to make a final legal finding that any specific prosecutor committed misconduct.

## What the Exhibit Shows

The discovery exhibit shows that P.S. sent an email to YWCA's general address on August 14, 2018 complaining about E'Lise Chard's conduct toward Elvis Nuno and describing Elvis as kind, generous, respectful, and non-abusive. The same exhibit shows E'Lise Chard forwarding that email to Detective Arianna Adams and Kim Harvey with the note: "Here is an email to the YWCA, about me, on Elvis' behalf. Not sure if you want this, since it's purportedly not from Elvis, but I figured I'd send it anyway."

Kim Harvey then forwarded the chain to Arianna Adams on August 23, 2018, preserving it inside a county or prosecution-adjacent record stream. On the face of the document, that means favorable and contradictory information did not remain only inside YWCA; it moved into channels connected to the criminal case.

## Why This Matters Publicly

For public readers, the significance is straightforward. If a case continues while the broader prosecution ecosystem has access to information that undermines a central complainant's narrative, that creates a reasonable question about how contradictory information was handled.

The P.S. email was favorable in at least two visible ways: it portrayed Elvis Nuno positively and it directly accused E'Lise Chard of causing harassment and trouble. It also had potential credibility value because E'Lise's forwarding note appears to reframe the message as something suspiciously connected to Elvis even while acknowledging uncertainty about that attribution.

## Relationship to the Larger Record

Existing chronology materials describe the Missoula matter as part of a broader pattern involving complaints to YWCA, police escalation, YWCA-linked conflicts, and eventual dismissal with prejudice after extended criminal proceedings. Those materials also describe concerns about structural overlap between YWCA, investigators, and prosecutors.

Within that larger pattern, the P.S. email matters because it appears to place favorable and contradictory information inside the same institutional channels through which the case continued. For public purposes, that makes the exhibit relevant to the question whether the prosecution process was evaluating the record neutrally or moving forward despite accumulating signs that the narrative was more contested than the charging posture suggested.

## Public Questions Raised

This document supports several narrow questions appropriate for a public evidentiary page.

- Did anyone in the prosecution chain treat this email as favorable or credibility-relevant information?
- Was the message disclosed, reviewed, or discussed in a way that affected charging, negotiation, or case-maintenance decisions?
- Did the forwarding note cause the message to be reframed as suspicious contact rather than evaluated as a complaint about E'Lise Chard?
- If the case continued without visible reassessment after receipt of this kind of contradictory information, what does that say about how the broader prosecution record was being handled?

These are accountability questions, not final accusations. Their importance comes from the existence of a concrete exhibit, not from speculation alone.

## Public Framing of Misconduct Concerns

For publication purposes, the strongest framing is restrained: the P.S. email appears to be a documented example of favorable and potentially impeaching information entering prosecution-adjacent channels while the case continued. That is enough to support a public question about whether contradictory information was appropriately recognized and weighed.

The forwarding language is part of what makes the exhibit notable. E'Lise characterized the message as being "on Elvis' behalf" while simultaneously acknowledging it was only "purportedly" so, which can reasonably be read as recasting independent third-party speech into something potentially attributable to Elvis himself. For public readers, that detail helps explain why the document may bear on concerns about witness framing, credibility handling, and the treatment of favorable information.

## What This Addendum Does Not Claim

This public addendum does not claim that the email alone proves a Brady violation, proves bad faith, or establishes personal misconduct by Brian Lowney on any specific date. It also does not attempt to resolve what any individual prosecutor personally saw, knew, or decided without the complete official record.

The narrower conclusion supported here is that the P.S. email is a documented item of favorable and contradictory information that appears to have entered the prosecution-adjacent record while the case continued, and that this is relevant to public review of the prosecution process and the handling of contested facts.
