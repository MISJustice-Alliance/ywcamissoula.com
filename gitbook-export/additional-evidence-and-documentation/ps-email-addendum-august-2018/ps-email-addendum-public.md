# Public Addendum: August 14, 2018 P.S. Email and Forwarding Chain

## Overview

This public addendum summarizes a discovery record showing that a third-party email sent to the general YWCA of Missoula address on August 14, 2018, criticizing employee E'Lise Chard and supporting Elvis Nuno, was forwarded by E'Lise Chard to Detective Arianna Adams and Kim Harvey instead of appearing to remain within an internal staff-accountability process. The document is relevant to the broader public-interest question of whether complaints about YWCA staff conduct were being handled as internal grievances or redirected into law-enforcement and prosecution-adjacent channels.

This version is revised for public evidentiary use and anonymizes the third-party sender as **P.S.** to reduce unnecessary disclosure of personal identifying information while preserving the substance of the exhibit. It is intended as a factual and analytical summary for public review, not as legal advice or a final adjudication of any person's liability.

## What the Record Shows

According to the discovery exhibit, Kim Harvey forwarded an email chain to Arianna Adams on August 23, 2018, and that chain originated from E'Lise Chard's August 14, 2018 message forwarding an email addressed to `ywca@ywcaofmissoula.org` with the subject line "Attn: Regarding conduct of employee E'Lise Chard." In her forwarding note, E'Lise wrote: "Here is an email to the YWCA, about me, on Elvis' behalf. Not sure if you want this, since it's purportedly not from Elvis, but I figured I'd send it anyway."

The underlying message from P.S. described Elvis Nuno as kind, respectful, generous, and non-abusive, stated that E'Lise Chard had been causing harassment and trouble to Elvis and his family, and asked the YWCA to address what was "really going on." In substance, the email functioned both as a character reference and as a complaint about employee conduct sent to the organization's general inbox.

## Why This Matters Publicly

The public significance of this exhibit is straightforward: it appears to show criticism of a YWCA employee being routed by that same employee into the criminal-investigative channel. That does not by itself prove institutional wrongdoing, but it does raise a legitimate public-interest question about whether complaints concerning staff conduct were screened neutrally, logged appropriately, and reviewed by independent leadership.

This issue carries additional weight when read alongside earlier documented complaints to YWCA leadership alleging misconduct by E'Lise Chard and Rebecca Pettit and requesting internal action. If multiple complaints reached YWCA from different sources, yet the visible record reflects escalation into police and prosecution systems rather than documented internal investigation, that pattern supports public scrutiny of YWCA's governance, grievance handling, and boundaries with law enforcement.

## Public-Interest Questions Raised

The exhibit raises several fact questions that remain unresolved on the current public record.

- Did YWCA executive or administrative leadership ever receive and review the original email sent to the general inbox?
- Was the message logged or processed as a personnel or grievance matter before it was forwarded externally?
- Were any internal steps taken to investigate the complaint about staff conduct?
- Why was a complaint addressed to YWCA apparently redirected into law-enforcement and victim-services channels by the employee who was the subject of the complaint?

These questions are appropriate subjects for public accountability because they concern process integrity, confidentiality boundaries, and whether a nonprofit advocacy organization functioned independently from police and prosecutors in a matter involving one of its own employees.

## Relationship to Broader Pattern

Existing chronology materials describe a broader pattern in which Elvis Nuno's formal complaints to YWCA were followed by no documented internal response, continued employment of the accused staff, and escalating police and prosecutorial activity involving YWCA-linked actors. Those materials also describe structural conflicts, including Detective Connie Brueckner's role on the YWCA board while police activity related to the same dispute intensified.

Within that larger pattern, the P.S. email is important because it shows a concrete mechanism rather than a general theory: a third-party complaint directed to YWCA about employee conduct appears in the available record as having been pulled into the prosecution-side information stream by E'Lise Chard herself. For public readers, that makes the concern easier to understand because it is not abstract; it is a documented forwarding chain.

## What This Addendum Does Not Claim

This public addendum does not claim that the exhibit alone proves a complete conspiracy, establishes criminal liability, or resolves all factual disputes about the Missoula matter. It also does not assume, without further records, that every YWCA leader or every prosecutor personally reviewed the email or made a specific decision based on it.

What it does support is a narrower and more defensible conclusion: a complaint and character reference sent to YWCA was forwarded by the accused employee to investigative recipients, and that forwarding is relevant to public evaluation of YWCA's complaint handling, neutrality, and relationship to law-enforcement actors in this case.
