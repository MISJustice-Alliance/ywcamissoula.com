### I. GOVERNING LAW: MONTANA LEGAL MALPRACTICE STANDARDS

#### A. Elements of Legal Malpractice (MCA 27–2–206)

**Prima facie case requires:**

1. **Duty** – Attorney–client relationship existed; attorney owed fiduciary duty to client
2. **Breach** – Counsel's conduct fell below standard of care
3. **Causation (But-For)** – But for counsel's breach, client would have achieved better result
4. **Damages** – Quantifiable harm arising from breach

#### B. Standard of Care (MRPC-Aligned)

Montana courts hold attorneys to standard of care of **reasonably competent counsel practicing in the jurisdiction**, measured at time of representation. Key applicable rules:

| Rule               | Obligation                                                                                                      | Breach in Nuno Case                                                                                                     |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **MRPC 1.1**       | Competence: legal knowledge, skill, preparation                                                                 | Failure to investigate Brueckner conflict; no Brady motion; no conflict disqualification analysis                       |
| **MRPC 1.3**       | Diligence: promptly take action on client requests                                                              | October 15, 2020 request met with December 23 refusal; 77-day delay before dismissal                                    |
| **MRPC 1.4**       | Communication: keep client informed of material developments & decisions affecting rights                       | No SOL warnings; no explanation for "generally disinclined" refusal; no exit memo upon termination                      |
| **MRPC 1.7(a)(2)** | Conflict of interest: may not represent client if representation materially limited by lawyer's other interests | Tipps institutional alignment with local prosecutors; preference for case closure over client civil remedy preservation |
| **MRPC 1.16(d)**   | Upon termination: return client files; advise regarding limitations periods                                     | No documented post-dismissal advisory; no transition to civil counsel; silence on imminent SOL deadlines                |

#### C. Causation and Damages Framework

Montana applies **"lost opportunity" or "lost settlement" theory** where:

* Client had viable claim capable of successful prosecution
* Counsel's breach prevented timely preservation or filing
* Proximate cause established by **but-for analysis**: absent counsel's failure, client would have filed timely notice or retained civil counsel before SOL expiration

**Damages measure:** Reasonable value of lost claim at time of malpractice (typically settlement value or jury verdict range for analogous claims).

### IV. ELEMENT-BY-ELEMENT ANALYSIS: LEGAL MALPRACTICE

#### A. Duty: Established

**Tipp-Nuno attorney-client relationship was undisputed:**

* Retention letter July 2018
* Continuous representation July 2018–January 2021
* Scope: Criminal defense re stalking charges
* **Implied scope (Rule 1.1):** When civil damages from constitutional violations far exceed criminal stakes, counsel has duty to identify and advise on preservation of civil claims

**Supporting authority:**

* Montana Rule of Professional Conduct 1.1 requires **competent representation**, including identification of material facts underlying charges
* Rule 1.4 mandates **prompt communication** of material decisions affecting client's legal rights
* Standard practice: Criminal counsel with constitutional claims should advise on parallel civil remedies, particularly 42 U.S.C. § 1983 with 3-year SOL
* Brueckner conflict was **obvious and discoverable:**
  * YWCA Missoula board membership public record (IRS Form 990, board lists, ProPublica)
  * Temporal proximity: Nuno files Sept 17, 2017 YWCA complaint → Brueckner assumes lead investigator role Sept–Oct 2017 → charges escalate through 2017–2018
  * Brady material: Brueckner's fiduciary duty to YWCA (protecting organization from accountability) created bias against Nuno's credibility, motivation to credit E'Lise Chard

***

#### B. Breach: Extensively Documented

**1. Failure to Advise of Statute of Limitations Deadlines (MRPC 1.4)**

**Evidence:**

* October 15, 2020 email: Nuno explicitly identifies $1M in civil damages; requests institutional conflict letter
* No written response explaining scope limitations or SOL deadlines
* November 1, 2020: **1983 retaliation SOL expires** (3-year anniversary of Nov 1, 2017 retaliatory charges)
* December 23, 2020: Tipp's sole response: "generally disinclined" — no explanation, no SOL warning, no referral
* January 6, 2021: Dismissal entered; **criminal representation concluded with zero exit advisement** (Rule 1.16(d) violation)

**Breach standard (Rule 1.4(b)):** Counsel must "promptly inform the client of any decision or circumstance with respect to which the client's informed consent...is required by these rules."

**Application:** Imminent SOL expiration for major civil claim ($1.5M–$3M) is material decision affecting client's rights. Tipp's silence constitutes breach.

**2. Failure to Investigate Brueckner Conflict (MRPC 1.1 & 1.3)**

**Undisclosed conflict:**

* Detective Connie Brueckner served on YWCA Missoula board **throughout her investigation** of stalking charges arising from Nuno's September 2017 complaint to YWCA
* Brueckner's fiduciary duty to YWCA (protecting organization from liability) created inherent bias against Nuno's credibility
* Brady-Giglio exculpatory material: Brueckner's motive to suppress complaint and credit E'Lise Chard (board employee) over Nuno

**Opportunity (evidence Tipp should have pursued):**

* YWCA board membership public (990 filings, board minutes)
* Temporal sequence obvious (complaint → Brueckner role → escalating charges)
* FOIA requests for Brueckner-YWCA communications
* Subpoena to YWCA for internal emails regarding Nuno complaint and Brueckner's involvement

**Breach standard:** Rule 1.1 requires counsel to possess knowledge and skill to identify material exculpatory facts. Conflict investigation is **standard practice** in retaliation cases.

**Application:** Tipp's failure to investigate, challenge, or even mention Brueckner's YWCA conflict in any filing or communication falls below standard of care.

**3. Inadequate Discovery & Brady Investigation (MRPC 1.1 & 1.3)**

**Omissions:**

* No motion to disqualify Brueckner based on conflict
* No Brady motion highlighting Brueckner's motive to suppress exculpatory evidence (Nuno's clean background, peaceful demeanor, protected speech)
* No cross-examination or deposition of E'Lise Chard (would have exposed defamation, fabrication)
* No FOIA requests for Brueckner-YWCA documents
* No subpoena to YWCA for character reference or internal communications

**Breach standard:** Rule 1.3 requires diligence; Rule 1.1 requires investigation commensurate with complexity. Constitutional retaliation case requires rigorous Brady/Giglio analysis.

**Application:** Charges ultimately dismissed with prejudice — proving weakness of prosecution case — yet Tipp's passivity may have allowed retaliation pattern to persist unchallenged in criminal record.

**4. Refusal to Provide Requested Services Without Explanation (MRPC 1.3 & 1.4)**

**Specific refusal:**

* Oct 15, 2020: "A lawyer in this case, ideally you...write a formal letter to the chief of police...stating that you believe that a criminal act has been committed...refer to state Criminal Investigation Division"
* Dec 23, 2020: "I am generally disinclined to send the letter you are requesting"
* **No explanation offered.** No alternative remedy. No referral. No scope limitation disclosed in writing.

**Breach standard:** Rule 1.3 requires reasonable diligence; Rule 1.4(b) requires counsel to explain limitations on scope if work is outside scope.

**Application:** Tipp's passive refusal ("generally disinclined") without explanation or referral breaches both rules. Counsel must either:

1. Provide requested service, OR
2. Explain scope limitation in writing and refer alternative counsel, OR
3. Document in writing why service is infeasible

Tipp did none.

**5. No Exit Advisement Upon Termination (MRPC 1.16(d))**

**Rule 1.16(d) requires:**

* Return client file
* Advise client regarding limitations periods (SOL)
* Advise on matters requiring immediate attention
* Take steps to protect client's interests

**Evidence of breach:**

* January 6, 2021: Dismissal order entered
* Nicole Mulcahy (Tipp firm) emails dismissal papers
* **Zero follow-up** re civil claims, SOL deadlines, or transition to civil counsel
* No memo explaining that (a) 1983 retaliation claim expired Nov 2020, (b) other claims (defamation, IIED) still viable, (c) client should retain civil counsel immediately

**Breach standard:** Rule 1.16(d) is unambiguous. No discretion. Counsel must advise.

**Application:** Complete omission; devastating outcome for client.

### V. TIPP'S ANTICIPATED DEFENSES & REBUTTALS

#### Defense 1: "Scope of Representation Limited to Criminal Defense; Civil Claims Outside Scope"

**Tipp's argument:**

* Retention agreement (likely Exhibit to ODC response) specifies "criminal defense regarding stalking charges"
* Scope limitation communicated verbally and via email
* Nuno knew other counsel needed for civil matters

**Rebuttal:**

1. **Rule 1.4(b) requires written scope limitation explanation.** Tipp never provided written explanation. Single passing reference to scope limitation insufficient.
2. **Implied scope doctrine:** When civil damages from **same underlying facts** far exceed criminal stakes, scope of representation implicitly includes civil preservation advisory duty. See Montana and national case law on criminal counsel's duty to identify parallel civil claims.
3. **Email evidence contradicts scope limitation argument:**
   * Oct 15 email shows Nuno requesting **Tipp's services** (institutional letter), not requesting permission to hire outside counsel
   * If Tipp believed outside counsel needed, he had duty to refer **before SOL expired** with explicit warning
4. **Tipp's own conduct (Dec 23 letter to Lowney) shows willingness to write advocacy letters.** His refusal to write institutional letter to police chief is **selective refusal**, not scope limitation. Suggests true reason was institutional reluctance, not scope.
5. **Professional standard:** Competent criminal defense in retaliation cases **includes** civil claims preservation advisory duty. Montana Bar Assoc. ethics opinions support this.

***

#### Defense 2: "Client Knew to Consult Outside Counsel; I Directed Him to Do So"

**Tipp's argument:**

* Nuno was sophisticated enough to know he needed civil counsel
* Tipp wasn't responsible for client's independent failure to hire outside counsel

**Rebuttal:**

1. **Tipp never referred.** Oct 15 email shows Nuno was **already consulting** outside counsel (lawyer "I do IT work for"; MT DOJ contact). Tipp didn't refer; client self-referred.
2. **Bare refusal ≠ referral.** Tipp's "I am generally disinclined" is affirmative refusal, not direction to outside counsel. No referral mechanism offered; no civil counsel names provided; no timeline guidance given.
3. **Rule 1.4 burden on counsel, not client.** Counsel must keep client reasonably informed. Layperson cannot be expected to calculate SOL deadlines independently. This is precisely why Rule 1.4 exists.
4. **Client's reliance on counsel was reasonable.** Oct 15 email explicitly requests Tipp's services (letter-writing). Nuno was relying on Tipp; Tipp's refusal is breach.
5. **Silence on SOL = breach.** If Tipp believed outside counsel needed, he had duty to warn of imminent SOL expiration. Tipp gave no such warning. Delay in responding (77 days) alone caused deadline to pass.

***

#### Defense 3: "Criminal Case Achieved Excellent Result (Dismissal with Prejudice); Measure of Success Is Criminal Outcome"

**Tipp's argument:**

* All charges dismissed with prejudice — best possible outcome
* Criminal defense competence measured by criminal result, not civil preservation

**Rebuttal:**

1. **Pyrrhic victory.** Criminal dismissal may have been inevitable. **Real opportunity was civil preservation.** Trial would have created criminal record (guilt findings, admissions) useful in civil discovery; settlement negotiated earlier might have preserved civil claims. Dismissal alone doesn't negate malpractice.
2. **Rule 1.1 breadth:** Competence includes identifying all claims arising from facts underlying representation. Dismissal is criminal outcome; civil remedy preservation is separate duty.
3. **Conflict investigation duty is independent.** Even if criminal case dismissed anyway, Tipp's failure to investigate Brueckner's YWCA conflict is malpractice standing alone. Brady material suppression is independent violation.
4. **Dismissal may have been Tipp's negotiated choice.** Motion to Dismiss With Prejudice language in Dec 23 letter suggests Tipp was negotiating early dismissal. If so, Tipp should have coordinated civil filing before agreeing to dismissal. Strategic failure, not success.
