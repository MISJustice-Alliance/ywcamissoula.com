# Officer Ethan Smith Complaint Analysis — March 2018

Officer Ethan Smith's repeated, unwanted contacts with Tyrone and Ellie Nuno in 2017–2018, culminating in a formal March 2018 complaint to the Missoula Police Department, provide a primary-source snapshot of how family harassment and informal "fishing expeditions" were used to build a stalking narrative around Elvis Nuno despite no concrete evidence of wrongdoing. This article is intended for the evidentiary archive to document that pattern and to situate the complaint in the broader context of YWCA–police coordination in Missoula.

## Purpose and Context

This article preserves and contextualizes a March 2018 complaint by Tyrone Nuno, father of telecom engineer Elvis Ryland Nuno, documenting alleged harassment by Missoula Police Department Officer Ethan Smith. The complaint date and content align with a broader pattern in which YWCA Missoula staff and MPD personnel coordinated sustained surveillance and contact of the Nuno family during the early "Montana phase" of the case.

According to internal research on this archive, YWCA employees E'Lise Chard and Rebecca Pettit had already enlisted Officer Smith in June 2017 to pursue an "elaborate cyber-crime spree" theory targeting Mr. Nuno, despite a record in Washington and Montana courts showing dismissals, evidentiary collapse, and protected First Amendment activity. Against that backdrop, the complaint by Mr. Nuno's father functions not as an isolated grievance, but as early corroboration of institutional patterns later documented in multiple civil-rights submissions and bar complaints.

## The March 2018 Complaint

In his March 2018 letter addressed to Sergeant Dustin Delridge at 435 Ryman Street, Missoula, Montana, Tyrone Nuno describes a sustained pattern of contact by Officer Ethan Smith beginning in June 2017, when Smith appeared unannounced at the residence of Mr. Nuno's ex-wife, Ellie, to ask questions about their son, Elvis. From that point forward, Tyrone reports that Officer Smith made "at least a half dozen" additional attempts to contact him and Ellie through visits, phone calls, messages, and letters.

Initially, Tyrone states he cooperated and "spoke with him briefly once," but emphasizes that the officer persisted "incessantly" in calling and leaving messages for both parents even after it was clear they had no relevant information. He characterizes this as "highly unprofessional and unethical conduct" for a police officer to target family members "over a matter they have nothing to do with," noting that the repeated contacts "cause a great deal of tension" in the family and amount to harassment.

## Tyrone Nuno's Characterization of Officer Smith's Intentions

In the core passage that defines the complaint's evidentiary value, Tyrone writes:

> "It seems to me like he is greatly exaggerating whatever this situation is and has no reason to believe my son is actually doing anything wrong, but is simply fishing for information and is trying to embarrass, humiliate, and intimidate my son."

This language does several things at once. First, it directly challenges the existence of any concrete basis for suspecting criminal conduct by Elvis, emphasizing that Officer Smith had "no reason to believe" wrongdoing had occurred. Second, it characterizes the officer's repeated outreach as a "fishing" exercise—unspecific, exploratory probing of family members, rather than targeted investigation based on probable cause.

Third, Tyrone identifies a psychological and reputational dimension: the repeated contacts appear aimed at embarrassing, humiliating, and intimidating his son by weaponizing his elderly parents and ex-wife as investigative tools. This allegation is consistent with later findings in the comprehensive Montana-phase report, which describes Officer Smith's "off-record, aggressive, and intimidating phone calls" to the Nuno parents and notes that their distress contributed to his mother's eventual involuntary mental-health commitment.

## Demand for Cease-and-Desist

Tyrone concludes his complaint by expressly demanding that MPD cease all attempts to contact him and his family:

> "Please, I am asking that I be left alone, and that all attempts to contact myself and my family members cease and desist immediately."

He states that he wants to be "left alone" because he "doesn't know anything" about the alleged situation, and that the pressure has left him "incredibly angered" by what he calls Officer Smith's "unbecoming behavior and incessant harassment." This explicit cease-and-desist request is important for later legal analysis because it confirms the family's non-consent to further contact and clearly notifies the department of the distress caused by Smith's conduct.

Subsequent internal summaries in this archive indicate that, following the complaint and "subsequent conversations" between Tyrone and Sergeant Delridge, Officer Smith was removed from any case involving Mr. Nuno and E'Lise Chard based on the "appearance of impropriety and unbecoming conduct" toward the Nuno parents. That reassignment is a tacit institutional acknowledgement that the complaint was credible enough to warrant intervention.

## Relationship to YWCA–MPD Coordination

The broader record shows that by June 2017, YWCA staff had already initiated contact with Missoula PD and specifically with Officer Smith, leveraging his position to advance a narrative about Elvis Nuno as a dangerous cyber-stalker. Elvis's June 2018 letter to the YWCA president documents how staff "knowingly and repeatedly made false statements" and presented "fabricated evidence" to Officer Smith, including attempts to use his IT career as proof of an "elaborate cyber crime spree."

The Montana-phase report notes that Smith's subsequent harassment of the Nuno parents—including repeated calls and off-the-record contacts—prompted the family to file the very complaint summarized here. It further observes that despite Smith's removal, his investigative work product remained embedded in later stalking charging documents, including claims about alleged conversations with Tyrone and Elvis's mother that the parents categorically deny ever occurred.

Those later charging documents reportedly attribute statements to the Nuno parents implying that Elvis had mental-health issues and certain technical capabilities, assertions the parents explicitly refuted, suggesting that either the officer fabricated the conversation or prosecutors misrepresented it. The March 2018 complaint thus serves as contemporaneous evidence that the family viewed Smith's contacts as unwanted, intrusive, and baseless long before those contested statements appeared in official court filings.

## Institutional Significance

From a civil-rights and institutional-accountability perspective, Tyrone Nuno's complaint illustrates several recurring themes in this archive:

- Use of family surveillance as an investigative shortcut, particularly targeting elderly parents and ex-spouses who have "nothing to do with" the alleged conduct.
- Blurring of lines between formal investigation and informal harassment, with officers making repeated "off-record" calls and visits without clear legal basis.
- Collaboration between a nominally independent victim-services organization (YWCA Missoula) and law enforcement, where staff-originated narratives drive police contact and reputational harm.

The internal legal analysis included in this archive identifies such patterns as potential evidence of 42 U.S.C. § 1983 violations based on deliberate indifference, conflict-infected investigations, and unequal treatment of accused men in domestic-violence contexts. While only a licensed attorney can evaluate specific claims, the complaint by Tyrone Nuno is a crucial primary document that anchors those broader allegations to a specific officer, timeframe, and set of behaviors.
