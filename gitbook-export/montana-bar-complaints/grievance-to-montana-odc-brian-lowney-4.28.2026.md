## I. PRELIMINARY STATEMENT AND NOTICE OF PRIOR COMPLAINT

Grievant Elvis Ryland Nuno hereby files this formal grievance against Deputy County Attorney Brian Lowney, Esq. and the Missoula County Attorney's Office arising from the prosecution of misdemeanor and felony stalking charges that were dismissed with prejudice in January 2021 — after eighteen months of prosecution that produced no credible evidence of any criminal conduct.

Grievant wishes to note at the outset that he has previously attempted to notify the Missoula County Attorney's Office directly of the misconduct described herein. That effort resulted in a categorical refusal to hear, review, or investigate any complaint. No internal corrective action was taken. The prosecutorial office has demonstrated no willingness to police its own conduct. Accordingly, this grievance is submitted to ODC as the proper independent oversight authority, and is expressly intended to create an administrative record of exhaustion of all available non-judicial avenues of accountability prior to civil action.

This grievance is filed pursuant to the Montana Rules for Lawyer Disciplinary Enforcement and alleges violations of the following Montana Rules of Professional Conduct:

- **Rule 3.1** — Meritorious Claims and Contentions
- **Rule 3.4(a)** — Fairness to Opposing Party; Suppression of Evidence
- **Rule 3.8(a)** — Special Responsibilities of a Prosecutor (probable cause)
- **Rule 3.8(d)** — Special Responsibilities of a Prosecutor (disclosure of exculpatory evidence)
- **Rule 8.4(c)** — Misconduct (dishonesty, fraud, deceit, or misrepresentation)
- **Rule 8.4(d)** — Misconduct prejudicial to the administration of justice

## II. STATEMENT OF FACTS

### A. Background and the Relationship Between the Parties

The stalking charges filed in September 2018 arose from a complaint made by E'Lise Chard, an Operations Manager at the YWCA of Missoula. The fundamental and unrebutted fact — that E'Lise Chard and Elvis Nuno were, in every meaningful sense, strangers — was never investigated, never acknowledged in any charging document, and was actively suppressed throughout the prosecution.

The totality of the documented relationship between E'Lise Chard and Mr. Nuno consisted of:

1. A brief hostile introduction in approximately 2001, during which she stated she was "obligated to hate anyone [her sister] dates";
2. A single phone call in February 2015 in which Mr. Nuno contacted her to arrange safe housing for her estranged sister Danielle, who had become abusive in Mr. Nuno's home;
3. Several phone calls, emails, and one letter over approximately eight months in 2015, in which Mr. Nuno expressed serious concern for Danielle's deteriorating mental health and urged E'Lise to assist her sister;
4. Three brief, non-threatening emails sent in 2017 in which Mr. Nuno demanded E'Lise cease harassment of himself and his family.

Had any member of the prosecution asked the single obvious threshold question — "In what way and for how long have you personally known this person?" — no probable cause for stalking charges could have survived even the most cursory review. There are baristas with whom Mr. Nuno has a more substantial personal history than with E'Lise Chard.

### B. The Prior Protected Speech Ruling That Was Ignored

On June 28, 2018, at the contested hearing on E'Lise Chard's petition for an order of protection in the Missoula Municipal Court, Judge Warren made an explicit finding from the bench that Mr. Nuno's formal complaint letter to the YWCA of Missoula — which had included a $400 donation as a gesture of good faith — constituted **protected First Amendment speech** and was **not a communication to E'Lise Chard** and **not a violation of any existing no-contact order**.

That ruling was on the record. Deputy County Attorney Brian Lowney and the Missoula County Attorney's Office were aware of it.

Despite this judicial determination, the September 25, 2018 charging documents filed by Brian Lowney (Motion and Affidavit for Leave to File Information, Cause No. DC-18-587) relied heavily on the YWCA letter and associated conduct as grounds for filing one misdemeanor count and two felony counts of stalking. The prosecution did not disclose Judge Warren's ruling to the district court. It did not move to account for the First Amendment finding. It instead proceeded as though no such judicial determination existed, litigating for an additional eighteen months before the charges were finally dismissed with prejudice in January 2021.

The continued use of conduct already adjudicated as constitutionally protected as the basis for felony prosecution violates due process, constitutes abuse of process, and reflects bad faith prosecution that is directly inconsistent with Rule 3.8(a) and Rule 3.1.

### C. The Charging Documents Relied on Officer Ethan Smith After His Removal for "Obvious Impropriety"

Prior to September 2018, Mr. Nuno's parents, Ty and Ellie Nuno, filed a formal complaint regarding Officer Ethan Smith of the Missoula Police Department, citing his documented close personal relationship with E'Lise Chard and his pattern of sending repeated waves of privacy-in-communications citations against Mr. Nuno without credible independent investigation. That complaint was acted upon: Officer Smith's commanding officer identified an "obvious appearance of impropriety" and formally removed Officer Smith from any case involving E'Lise Chard and Mr. Nuno.

This removal — and the reason for it — was known to, or discoverable by, the Missoula County Attorney's Office at the time charges were filed.

Nevertheless, the September 25, 2018 charging affidavit signed by Brian Lowney is built almost entirely on Officer Ethan Smith's reports. The affidavit cites Smith's investigative work, his characterizations of Mr. Nuno's conduct, and his conversations purportedly with Mr. Nuno's parents. Both parents have explicitly denied that the conversation Smith claimed to have had with them regarding Mr. Nuno's phone number and "technical capabilities" ever occurred. The affidavit also attributes a statement about mental health issues to Mr. Nuno's mother — a statement she has categorically denied making.

Prosecutorial reliance on reports from an officer removed for obvious impropriety, without disclosure of that removal, implicates Brady v. Maryland (373 U.S. 83, 1963), Rule 3.8(d), and Rule 8.4(c). The failure to disclose Officer Smith's removal was material: it denied both the defense and the court the ability to assess the credibility and integrity of the evidentiary foundation for the charges.

### D. Detective Connie Brueckner's Undisclosed Conflict of Interest

Following Officer Ethan Smith's removal, the case was reassigned to Detective Connie Brueckner. Detective Brueckner was, at the time, a sitting **Board Member at Large** of the YWCA of Missoula — the same organization whose employee, E'Lise Chard, was the complaining witness, and whose institutional conduct was the subject of Mr. Nuno's protected complaint letter.

Detective Brueckner conducted the investigation that led to the escalated stalking charges. She obtained and executed the search warrant that resulted in the September 2018 raid of Mr. Nuno's home during a professional work meeting, the seizure of all of his work equipment and personal electronics, and charges carrying a $50,000 bail. She served as the officer of record for that warrant.

This conflict — a police investigator with a fiduciary and institutional relationship to the organization whose employee she was investigating — was never disclosed to defense counsel or to the court. It constitutes a material Brady violation. The prosecution either knew of Detective Brueckner's board membership and withheld it, or failed to conduct even minimal vetting of the investigator assigned to a case with obvious institutional-alignment risks. Either scenario is inconsistent with the obligations imposed by Rule 3.8(d) and Rule 3.4(a).

### E. E'Lise Chard's On-the-Record Admissions Were Suppressed

At the June 28, 2018 protection order hearing before Judge Warren, Judge Warren asked E'Lise Chard directly — not once, but **twice** — whether Mr. Nuno had made any attempt to contact her beyond three emails sent over a year prior. Both times, E'Lise Chard answered **no**. She affirmatively stated on the record that he had made no attempts to contact her in person, had sent only two to three brief emails requesting she cease harassment, and had not made any threatening communications.

At the same hearing, Mr. Nuno stated on the record that he had voluntarily agreed to the no-contact order and had absolutely no intention or desire to have any contact with E'Lise Chard whatsoever. This was consistent with the documentary record.

These sworn on-the-record statements by the complaining witness herself were directly exculpatory: they negated the "course of conduct" element required under Montana's stalking statute, Mont. Code Ann. § 45-5-220, and established on the record that no reasonable basis for fear existed.

The Missoula County Attorney's Office was present for, or had access to, these proceedings. The prosecution subsequently filed three counts of stalking — including two felonies — without disclosing, accounting for, or grappling with the fact that the complaining witness had twice sworn under oath that no meaningful contact had occurred. This omission is inconsistent with Rule 3.8(d) and Rule 3.1.

### F. Suppression of Exculpatory Character Evidence

The YWCA of Missoula received a written character reference letter — addressed to the organization — from an individual, referred to here as "Witness P.S.," who had been personally housed and supported by Mr. Nuno at no cost when she was homeless and pregnant. In that letter, Witness P.S.:

- Described Mr. Nuno as consistently non-violent and helpful to others;
- Stated that during times of conflict, he handled himself by working through issues rather than escalating them — directly contradicting the "dangerous stalker" narrative advanced by E'Lise Chard;
- Expressed her objection to E'Lise Chard's conduct, stating she hated "what your employee E'Lise Chard has been doing to him for no reason at all";
- Called on the YWCA to address the issue.

The YWCA did not forward this letter to the prosecution or to defense counsel. Whether the prosecution independently received, possessed, or was on notice of this exculpatory material has not been established; however, the prosecution's coordination with YWCA-affiliated actors throughout the case — and its failure to conduct any meaningful independent investigation of E'Lise Chard's credibility — raises serious concerns about whether this material was either obtained and suppressed or deliberately not sought.

### G. Speculative and Constitutionally Infirm Charging Theories

The September 25, 2018 charging affidavit signed by Brian Lowney contains evidentiary propositions that, individually and collectively, fail to meet the threshold of probable cause and reflect the use of prejudicial speculation as a substitute for evidence:

1. **ProtonMail as evidence of criminal intent.** The affidavit characterizes Mr. Nuno's use of ProtonMail — a lawful, widely used encrypted email service — as evidence consistent with stalking, quoting it as "the Swiss bank account of email services." This insinuation treats use of a privacy tool as evidence of criminality and reflects a prejudicial, legally unfounded theory that would chill protected First and Fourth Amendment-related conduct.
2. **Third-person text messages attributed without forensic support.** E'Lise Chard reported receiving text messages she believed were from Mr. Nuno because they were "written in the third person" and "sounded like" him. No forensic analysis, no metadata, no carrier records, and no expert testimony linked these messages to Mr. Nuno. The affidavit treats subjective impressions as if they constituted evidence. The prosecution proceeded on this basis for eighteen months without obtaining forensic support.
3. **IT profession as evidence of malicious capability.** The affidavit infers from Mr. Nuno's career as an information technology engineer a special capacity to evade detection or circumvent protective measures. No technical expert was ever engaged. The services cited in the affidavit — Guerrilla Mail, Pinger — are publicly available to any internet user and require no specialized knowledge. Using a defendant's lawful profession as evidence of criminal intent violates basic evidentiary standards and is constitutionally suspect.
4. **Reliance on a witness who did not want further involvement.** The affidavit relies in part on Rebecca Pettit (referred to as "Friend 1"), a subordinate of E'Lise Chard at the YWCA, to support the harassment narrative. At the time charges were filed, Rebecca Pettit had already stated on the record that she did not want further involvement in the matter and did not feel threatened or harassed in the manner E'Lise Chard claimed. The prosecution continued to use her as a pillar of the charging theory while failing to disclose her expressed reluctance and recantation of the fear narrative.

These are not minor evidentiary disputes. They represent a pattern of using speculation, innuendo, and technically misleading characterizations in a sworn charging affidavit, in violation of Rules 3.1 and 8.4(c).

### H. The Arraignment Comparison: Disparate and Retaliatory Bail Treatment

At Mr. Nuno's arraignment following the September 2018 raid, the individual arraigned immediately before him had been charged with **domestic violence assault involving choking his wife with a chain while holding her at gunpoint**. That defendant received a significantly lower bail amount than was sought for Mr. Nuno, and the court and prosecution took care to ensure that the arrangement would allow that defendant to keep his job as a liquor deliveryman.

When Mr. Nuno's case was called, the prosecution described him to the court as one of the most violent and dangerous individuals to have come through the facility in recent history — and sought bail of $50,000 (later attempting to raise it to $100,000, and later still attempted to revoke bail entirely) — for charges that were, at their factual core, premised on a polite letter and a $400 donation to a nonprofit.

At that first meeting following arraignment, Mr. Nuno's own defense counsel, Bryan Tipp, observed that Mr. Nuno had "obviously made the wrong people very angry" — a statement that reflects contemporaneous recognition, even by counsel, that the prosecution's conduct was driven by institutional animus rather than evidence of genuine dangerousness.

The disparity in treatment at arraignment, when placed alongside all other facts in this grievance, is consistent with a prosecution that was not calibrated to the evidence but to the protection of institutional interests connected to the YWCA of Missoula and its affiliated actors.

### I. Repeated Filing, Dismissal, Re-Filing, and Coercive Bail Escalation

Between June 2018 and January 2021, the Missoula County Attorney's Office engaged in a pattern of filing charges, dismissing them, and re-filing — each cycle associated with escalating bail demands and restrictive conditions — during a period in which no new precipitating conduct had occurred.

The specific pattern included:

- Initial misdemeanor stalking and criminal defamation charges filed following the YWCA letter;
- Escalation to three stalking counts (one misdemeanor, two felonies) following the reassignment to Detective Brueckner and a warrant executed during Mr. Nuno's workday;
- Repeated arrests during the pre-trial period based on alleged technical violations;
- A bail revocation motion filed after Mr. Nuno inadvertently traveled within a narrow exclusion zone while already wearing a GPS ankle monitor and traveling with his father;
- The GPS ankle monitor itself restricting movement to Missoula County and requiring regular meetings with a pretrial services officer for approximately eighteen months.

Each re-filing reset conditions, raised bail, and — consistent with the prosecution's stated interest in maintaining pre-trial custody as leverage — was not accompanied by any new evidence of threatening conduct, in-person contact, or genuine dangerousness. The explicit purpose of the bail revocation motion appears to have been to secure pretrial incarceration as a means of coercing a guilty plea.

This pattern directly caused the loss of Mr. Nuno's $150,000-per-year IT consulting contract. He was repeatedly removed from work without notice, unable to explain his absences to clients, and eventually terminated from the contract. The prosecution was aware of these consequences and continued its conduct regardless.

Using the criminal charging and bail process as leverage to compel a plea, in the absence of genuine probable cause, constitutes abuse of process and conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice under Rule 8.4(d).

### J. Deferred Prosecution Agreement: Surrendering First Amendment Rights as a Condition of Dismissal

The charges were ultimately resolved through a Deferred Prosecution Agreement (DPA) that conditioned dismissal upon Mr. Nuno's agreement to remove his protected speech — specifically, his formal complaint to the YWCA — from public internet platforms, to remain silent on related matters for twelve months, and to agree to conditions that effectively validated the prosecution's theory of the case while avoiding a judicial determination on the merits.

This outcome — achieved only after eighteen months of prosecution that produced no evidence of actual stalking conduct — required Mr. Nuno to surrender his First Amendment rights as the price of avoiding continued prosecution. The American Bar Association, in Resolution 113E (2018), formally condemned the practice of conditioning dismissal or plea agreements on defendants' waiver of civil rights, recognizing that such agreements undermine constitutional protections, prevent institutional accountability, and serve institutional interests over the interests of justice.

Judge Warren had already ruled that the YWCA complaint was protected speech. The prosecution's use of the DPA to condition dismissal on the removal of that same speech, eighteen months later, transformed a judicial determination of constitutional protection into an extortionate mechanism. This is inconsistent with the duties imposed by Rule 3.8 and with the fundamental ethical obligation to seek justice rather than merely convictions.

### K. DPA-Period Harassment: Silenced Complainant, Unrestrained Attackers

The DPA imposed a twelve-month speech restriction on Mr. Nuno. During that same twelve-month period — while Mr. Nuno was legally prohibited from publicly responding, correcting the record, or defending himself — individuals directly connected to E'Lise Chard and the YWCA of Missoula engaged in a sustained, coordinated campaign of defamation and business harassment against Mr. Nuno and his nonprofit internet service provider, Pacific NW Rural Broadband Alliance.

The pattern of conduct, which Brian Lowney was aware of and allowed to continue without intervention, included:

1. **Public social media defamation.** Individuals connected to E'Lise Chard posted publicly on social media identifying Mr. Nuno as a "stalker and a woman beater." These posts were false, defamatory, and made during the precise period in which the DPA contractually prevented Mr. Nuno from responding.
2. **Targeted attacks on the nonprofit.** The same individuals publicly insinuated that Mr. Nuno's operation of a rural internet service provider was itself suspect — specifically suggesting that his role as an ISP operator was cover for invading the privacy of the organization's customers. This was calculated to destroy both the professional and reputational foundation of a legitimate nonprofit serving rural communities.
3. **Direct private messaging to clients and prospective customers.** At least one individual — later identified as **Tyleen Root**, a client of the YWCA of Missoula with a close personal association with E'Lise Chard, and a person Mr. Nuno has never met, had any contact with, or responded to despite her prior attempts to contact him — directly messaged individuals who had interacted with the nonprofit's Facebook page. Ms. Root sent these individuals jail booking photographs of Mr. Nuno and fabricated narratives claiming he was stalking individuals he had never met and who had never attempted to make contact with him through any channel. Mr. Nuno became aware of these direct-message campaigns only after a prospective customer contacted him to ask why he was receiving these messages and what they meant.
4. **Law enforcement response: Dillon PD acted; Missoula did not.** Upon discovering the initial harassing posts made by Ms. Root on the nonprofit's public Facebook page, Mr. Nuno was able to identify her location as Dillon, Montana. He contacted the Dillon Police Department and spoke with Officer Ian Turnes. Officer Turnes immediately recognized Ms. Root — not only from the complaint but from multiple prior reports from other individuals regarding similar harassing behavior, and from her active status in the child welfare system: Ms. Root was at that time subject to a third custody dispute, had already had **two children removed from her custody** due to her criminal history and repeated incidents of neglect and abuse, and was actively evading law enforcement. Officer Turnes went out of his way to locate Ms. Root through Child Protective Services, approached her directly, and demanded she remove the offending posts. She complied. Officer Turnes explicitly warned her that continued conduct of this nature would result in criminal charges being filed.
    
    Ms. Root, who regularly changes her location, subsequently resumed the harassment. Mr. Nuno reported this continued conduct to the Missoula Police Department, to the Missoula County Attorney's Office, and to his own defense counsel Bryan Tipp. All three refused to take any action and provided no substantive response. The contrast is stark and instructive: a small-town police officer in Dillon, Montana — with no institutional stake in the matter — responded to a single complaint by independently investigating, locating the subject through CPS records, compelling compliance, and issuing a credible deterrent warning. The Missoula County Attorney's Office, the Missoula Police Department, and defense counsel Bryan Tipp, each with direct knowledge of the harassment and its connection to the complaining witness in Mr. Nuno's case, did nothing. This institutional non-response, during the period in which the DPA prevented Mr. Nuno from publicly defending himself, reflects a pattern of selective enforcement and deliberate indifference to Mr. Nuno's rights that is inseparable from the broader prosecutorial misconduct described in this grievance.
    
5. **Interference with media coverage.** Ms. Root or individuals in coordination with her contacted a radio station that had recently broadcast an interview with Mr. Nuno, making accusations to the station that Mr. Nuno was stalking them — a claim which was false and which Mr. Nuno was prohibited by the DPA from publicly rebutting.
6. **Disinformation to the public.** Multiple prospective customers reported having heard that Mr. Nuno was "running the organization as a scam" and "fleeing from federal charges." No federal charges existed, no charges related to fraud or financial misconduct existed, and the stalking charges that had been filed had been dismissed. These characterizations were demonstrably false and were circulated during the window in which the DPA prevented Mr. Nuno from defending himself.

Brian Lowney knew or had reason to know of this harassment campaign, its direct connection to E'Lise Chard and the YWCA of Missoula, and its timing relative to the DPA's speech restriction. He took no action to notify the court, to seek modification of the DPA's one-sided conditions, or to refer the conduct of Ms. Root or others to law enforcement for investigation. The practical effect of the DPA — as allowed to operate in the real world by the responsible prosecutor — was to impose a unilateral speech prohibition on Mr. Nuno while his accusers and their associates operated without constraint, defaming him to customers, media, and the public with no consequence.

This asymmetry was not incidental. A prosecutor who seeks a DPA condition that silences a defendant, and who then allows known retaliatory harassment to continue during that silence period without intervention, has used the legal process to facilitate the very harm the First Amendment is designed to prevent. This conduct implicates Rule 3.8(d) (failure to act on exculpatory and mitigating circumstances bearing on the fairness of the DPA conditions), Rule 8.4(c) (constructive misrepresentation to the court about the fairness and symmetry of the agreement's operation), and Rule 8.4(d) (conduct prejudicial to the administration of justice).

## III. RULES OF PROFESSIONAL CONDUCT IMPLICATED

### Rule 3.1 — Meritorious Claims and Contentions

Montana RPC 3.1 prohibits a lawyer from bringing a proceeding unless there is a basis in law and fact for doing so that is not frivolous. The charging theory here — that a polite letter with a donation, sent to an organization rather than to E'Lise Chard, and already ruled to be protected speech by a sitting judge, constituted felony stalking — lacked a good-faith factual basis. The prosecution continued that theory for eighteen months.

### Rule 3.4(a) — Suppression of Evidence

Montana RPC 3.4(a) prohibits a lawyer from unlawfully obstructing access to evidence or suppressing evidence that the lawyer has a legal obligation to reveal or produce. The non-disclosure of (1) Officer Smith's removal for impropriety, (2) Detective Brueckner's YWCA board membership, and (3) E'Lise Chard's on-the-record admissions constitute suppression of material exculpatory and impeachment evidence.

### Rule 3.8(a) — Special Responsibilities of a Prosecutor: Probable Cause

Montana RPC 3.8(a) requires that a prosecutor refrain from prosecuting a charge the prosecutor knows is not supported by probable cause. Given that the complaining witness had twice stated under oath that there had been no threatening contact, that all actual contact consisted of a handful of emails over a multi-year period, and that the parties were strangers with no substantial personal relationship, no reasonable prosecutor applying the objective probable cause standard could have concluded that the statutory elements of a "course of conduct" causing fear were met.

### Rule 3.8(d) — Special Responsibilities of a Prosecutor: Disclosure

Montana RPC 3.8(d) requires a prosecutor to make timely disclosure to the defense of all evidence or information known to the prosecutor that tends to negate the guilt of the accused or mitigate the offense. Officer Smith's removal, Detective Brueckner's YWCA conflict, E'Lise Chard's own contrary sworn testimony, and the character reference suppressed by the YWCA are all categories of material that a prosecutor with knowledge of them was obligated to disclose.

### Rule 8.4(c) — Dishonesty, Fraud, Deceit, or Misrepresentation

Montana RPC 8.4(c) makes it professional misconduct for a lawyer to engage in conduct involving dishonesty, fraud, deceit, or misrepresentation. The inclusion in the charging affidavit of statements attributed to Mr. Nuno's parents that they have categorically denied making, and the characterization of lawful privacy tools and professional qualifications as evidence of criminal intent, reflect misrepresentation in a sworn court filing.

### Rule 8.4(d) — Conduct Prejudicial to the Administration of Justice

Montana RPC 8.4(d) makes it professional misconduct to engage in conduct that is prejudicial to the administration of justice. The pattern of repeated filings, bail escalation, and pretrial revocation motions — not correlated with any new evidence of dangerousness — used the machinery of criminal prosecution as a coercive tool. Combined with a DPA that required surrender of protected speech as a condition of case resolution, this pattern is prejudicial to the administration of justice in a systemic and institutional sense.

## Legal Analysis and Case Significance

This grievance deepens and extends the institutional accountability record first opened by ODC 25-147 against defense counsel Bryan Tipp. Where the Tipp complaint documented how a single attorney's negligence and active discouragement cost a defendant his civil rights, the Lowney grievance reveals the prosecutorial side of the same machinery: a Deputy County Attorney who filed, escalated, and repeatedly re-filed stalking charges that no reasonable review of the record could support, while systematically withholding exculpatory and impeachment material from the court and defense.

**Connection to ODC 25-147 and institutional failure.** Both complaints arise from the same underlying prosecution—Cause No. DC-18-587 and its municipal-court predecessor TK-620-2017-11037—yet they expose different failure modes within the same institutional ecosystem. Tipp's alleged malpractice left constitutional violations unlitigated and deadlines unmet; Lowney's alleged misconduct fabricated and sustained a criminal case after a municipal judge had already ruled the central conduct constitutionally protected. Read together, the two grievances describe a justice system in Missoula that failed at both the defense and prosecution poles, with no internal corrective mechanism intervening at any stage.

**Prosecutorial misconduct and Brady violations.** The Lowney grievance identifies at least three categories of suppressed evidence. First, Officer Ethan Smith's removal for an “obvious appearance of impropriety”—his close personal relationship with complaining witness E'Lise Chard—was never disclosed to the district court, even though the charging affidavit Lowney signed relies almost entirely on Smith's reports. Second, Detective Connie Brueckner's simultaneous service as a YWCA Board Member at Large created a direct institutional conflict: the investigator had a fiduciary relationship with the organization whose employee she was charged with protecting. Third, E'Lise Chard's own sworn admissions at the June 28, 2018 protection order hearing—that Mr. Nuno had made no threatening contact, had sent only a few brief emails, and that the parties were effectively strangers—were directly exculpatory as to the “course of conduct” and fear elements of Montana's stalking statute. Each category triggers Brady v. Maryland and Montana RPC 3.8(d).

**The DPA speech-waiver issue.** Perhaps the most consequential legal theory in the grievance is the attack on the Deferred Prosecution Agreement's conditions. Judge Warren had already ruled from the bench that Mr. Nuno's YWCA complaint letter was protected First Amendment speech. Eighteen months later, Lowney conditioned dismissal on Mr. Nuno's removal of that same speech from public platforms and his agreement to remain silent for twelve months. The American Bar Association, in Resolution 113E (2018), formally condemned prosecutorial conditioning of dismissals on waivers of civil rights. Where a court has already ruled the speech protected, the prosecutor's subsequent use of that speech as a bargaining chip transforms a constitutional shield into a coercive lever—a practice the ABA recognized as undermining both individual rights and institutional accountability.

**DPA-period harassment and institutional complicity.** The grievance documents a sustained campaign of defamation and business interference conducted by individuals connected to E'Lise Chard and the YWCA during the exact twelve-month window in which the DPA silenced Mr. Nuno. Tyleen Root—a YWCA client with a documented history of child endangerment and custodial removal, whom Mr. Nuno had never met—distributed jail booking photographs and fabricated stalking narratives directly to Mr. Nuno's customers and media contacts. When Dillon Police Officer Ian Turnes learned of Root's conduct, he independently located her through Child Protective Services records, compelled removal of the offending posts, and issued a credible warning. By contrast, the Missoula County Attorney's Office, the Missoula Police Department, and Mr. Nuno's own defense counsel all declined to act. The asymmetry is stark: a small-town officer with no institutional stake enforced the law; the very prosecutor who had imposed the speech restriction allowed the retaliatory harassment to continue unchecked.

Together, these elements frame the Lowney grievance not merely as a disciplinary complaint against one prosecutor, but as evidentiary support for a broader pattern of institutional capture—one in which law enforcement, prosecutorial, and nonprofit actors operated in alignment to suppress a complainant's rights while shielding affiliated actors from consequence.
