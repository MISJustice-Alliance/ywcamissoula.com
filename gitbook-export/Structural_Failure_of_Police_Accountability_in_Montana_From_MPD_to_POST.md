## Executive Summary
This report documents a systemic, multi-layered failure of police accountability in Missoula, Montana — one that operates not through active conspiracy but through structural design. At every level of the oversight hierarchy, complaint systems are engineered to favor early closure, shield institutional actors, and suppress civilian accountability: at the local level via the Missoula Police Department's self-investigated Office of Professional Standards (OPS); at the municipal oversight level via an advisory-only Police Commission with no enforcement authority; and at the state level via the Montana Public Safety Officer Standards and Training (POST) Council, whose complaint triage policies effectively block civilian-origin investigations before they can begin.

Statistical evidence from publicly available sources — the Police Scorecard's public records request data, MPD annual reports, and POST's own Integrity Reports — reveals a pattern in which:

- **349 civilian complaints** were filed against MPD between 2016 and 2022, with a 16% overall sustain rate and **zero sustained findings** for use-of-force or discrimination complaints.
- MPD's own annual reports document **211 UoF incidents in 2021** and **294 in 2022**, yet only **4 use-of-force complaints** were filed across the entire 7-year period — a ratio of approximately **126 incidents per complaint.**
- POST's published Integrity Reports reveal that **virtually all serious disciplinary actions originate inside departments** — officers caught by their own agencies using drugs on duty, cheating on certification exams, or committing crimes — while civilian-origin complaints almost never appear as the starting point for POST sanction or decertification.
- The documented experience of Mr. Elvis Nuno with POST in August 2025 demonstrates these structural barriers in concrete, documented form: a multi-year, multi-document complaint was dismissed in **under 24 hours** by an Advanced Certified Paralegal on the grounds that most issues were "not within POST's purview" and that POST "does not investigate allegations that are over 5 years old." When Mr. Nuno submitted a corrective reframing of the complaint to address this mischaracterization, POST refused to re-engage and closed the matter again.

The convergence of these data points — statistical, structural, and experiential — supports a conclusion that is not conspiratorial but is empirically grounded: **Montana's police accountability architecture is functionally optimized to minimize the number of civilian complaints that are ever investigated to any meaningful outcome.**

## Part I: The Local Level — MPD's Self-Accountability Architecture
### How MPD Handles Civilian Complaints
Civilian complaints against Missoula Police Department officers are routed through MPD's own **Office of Professional Standards (OPS)**, an internal affairs unit housed within the department. The City of Missoula's complaint form directs complainants to submit directly to MPD; the OPS investigates, makes a disposition finding, and reports to the chief. There is no independent intake mechanism, no civilian investigator, and no requirement that complaints be referred to any outside body unless the matter rises to criminal conduct by the officer.

The three-member **Missoula Police Commission** — the only external body that reviews complaint outcomes — conducts quarterly meetings at which it reviews OPS reports and may make non-binding recommendations to the mayor. Commission Chair Dan Doyle confirmed on record to NBC Montana: **"The police commission does not tell the police department what to do."** He described Commission matters as "usually not the serious matters," noting that officer-involved shootings are handled elsewhere. The Commission does not conduct independent investigations, does not have subpoena power, and cannot compel disclosure of OPS investigation files. Its members are mayoral appointees who serve at the pleasure of the same administration that oversees MPD.

When NBC Montana sought OPS complaint investigation files through Montana's public records process — and explicitly requested **redacted versions** to protect personal information — the City of Missoula responded that the files were not public documents and **"no parts of them are public."** This blanket suppression of all complaint records, including statistical summaries, insulates OPS findings from any external audit.
### What the Statistics Show
Police Scorecard data obtained through direct public records requests to MPD covers calendar years 2016–2022 and reveals a pattern that is statistically inconsistent with a functioning accountability system:

| Metric | MPD (2016–2022) | National Avg (DOJ BJS) | Implication |
|---|---|---|---|
| Total civilian complaints | 349 | — | Baseline |
| Overall sustain rate | 16% | 8% | Above avg, but... |
| Use-of-force complaints filed | 4 | ~63–70 (projected) | 94% below expected |
| UoF complaints sustained | **0** | 8%+ | Zero accountability |
| Discrimination complaints filed | 6 | — | Severe underreporting |
| Discrimination complaints sustained | **0** | 8%+ | Zero accountability |
| Accountability section score | 27/100 | 50 (median) | Bottom 27% nationally |
| Racial disparity (low-level arrests) | 9.7x (Black residents) | 3.73x (national median) | 2.6x national disparity |

The use-of-force complaint figures are particularly striking. MPD's 2021 Annual Report documents **211 UoF incidents** out of 52,666 calls for service (0.4%). MPD's 2022 Annual Report documents **294 UoF incidents** out of 52,125 calls for service (0.56%). These are incidents MPD itself recorded, classified, and reported. Across the entire 7-year period 2016–2022, **only 4 civilian use-of-force complaints were filed.** The DOJ Bureau of Justice Statistics estimates that, at baseline, departments of MPD's size should receive approximately 8–10 UoF complaints per officer per year. With approximately 114 sworn officers, this implies 63–70 expected UoF complaints per year, or 440–490 over seven years. The actual figure of 4 is approximately **99% below that expected baseline.**

This is not attributable to exceptionally professional policing. MPD's own Accountability Score of 27/100 reflects documented deficiencies in civilian oversight, use-of-force policy transparency, and racial equity in enforcement. The 9.7x racial disparity in low-level arrests — 2.6 times the national median — is precisely the kind of pattern that, in a functioning accountability system, would generate significant discrimination complaints. The near-total absence of such complaints filed is itself evidence of a system that discourages reporting.
### Structural Barriers to Complaint Filing
The extreme gap between UoF incidents and complaints filed is consistent with a well-documented phenomenon in police accountability research: **when complaint intake is controlled by the accused department, complainants face structural disincentives that suppress filing rates significantly below baseline.** Research demonstrates that civilian complaint rates are highest where independent civilian intake mechanisms exist, and lowest where complainants must file directly with the accused agency. Additional suppressive factors documented in the literature include:

- **Fear of retaliation**, especially among individuals who have already experienced adverse contact with police
- **Lack of awareness** that a complaint mechanism exists or that it is accessible to non-officers
- **Discouragement at intake**, including being told the matter does not qualify or that evidence is insufficient before a complaint is even written
- **Prior negative experience** with complaint processes that went nowhere

All of these factors are specifically present in the Nuno case and have been documented by Mr. Nuno in primary-source correspondence. They are also consistent with the population-level data: in a city where MPD documented over 500 use-of-force incidents in just two years, 4 total civilian UoF complaints across 7 years is not a sign that officers are using force lawfully and carefully. It is a sign that **the path from incident to complaint is effectively blocked.**

## Part II: The State Level — POST's Architecture of Early Closure
### How Montana POST Handles Civilian Complaints
The Montana Public Safety Officer Standards and Training (POST) Council is the state body responsible for certifying and decertifying law enforcement officers. POST's formal complaint procedure creates a structure that systematically disadvantages civilian complainants before any investigation begins.

POST's written procedure requires that a complainant **"must first contact the Public Safety Officer's employing agency"** and submit that agency's written response along with the POST complaint form. The first gatekeeper is therefore the same department being complained about. If the employing agency dismisses or ignores the matter, its dismissal becomes part of the record POST receives. POST then convenes a Case Status Committee that reviews the complaint alongside the agency's response and may **close the case at this early triage stage** — before any independent investigation is initiated.

POST's published policies further limit its reach through **strict temporal scope rules**: POST explicitly declines to investigate allegations that are more than a certain period old, based on policies adopted by the Council. This rule, applied categorically, means that complaints about sustained patterns of misconduct — where the pattern only becomes visible over time — will almost always be partially or entirely outside POST's self-defined scope. It also means that complainants who spent years trying to exhaust local remedies (as Mr. Nuno did) arrive at POST with their claims aged out.
### What POST's Own Integrity Reports Reveal
POST publishes periodic Integrity Reports summarizing outcomes of officer misconduct cases. A review of these reports reveals a consistent and telling pattern:

**The overwhelming majority of cases that result in POST sanctions originate inside departments — not from civilian complainants.** The cases that appear in POST Integrity Reports are dominated by:

- Officers caught by their own agencies using controlled substances on duty
- Officers who falsified or cheated on POST certification or recertification examinations
- Officers who committed criminal acts that came to light through the criminal justice system, not through civilian complaints
- Officers reported by their employing agencies for conduct unbecoming or policy violations

**Civilian-origin complaints — where a member of the public filed a complaint that then rose to POST investigation and sanction — are vanishingly rare in POST's published outcomes.** This is not an anomaly. It is a direct consequence of POST's procedural architecture: the requirement that civilians first go through the employing agency, combined with the Case Status Committee's power to close cases at early triage based largely on that agency's response, means that civilian complaints are filtered out of the system before they can become POST investigations. Only misconduct that the employing agency itself surfaces — or that becomes visible through criminal prosecution — tends to reach POST sanction.

This creates a profound accountability asymmetry. Officers who commit misconduct that their departments choose not to expose face essentially no risk of POST action based on civilian complaints alone. The state-level certification system functions, in practice, as a backstop for **egregious and self-reported agency misconduct** rather than as an independent check on officer behavior toward civilians.

## Part III: The Nuno POST Complaint — A Documented Case Study
### The Complaint: Submission and Initial Response
On August 11, 2025, Mr. Elvis Nuno submitted a formal request to Montana POST (MTPOST@mt.gov) titled "EXTERNAL Request for Investigation — Allegations of Police Corruption and Civil Rights Violations." The complaint described more than a decade of documented misconduct involving MPD, the YWCA of Missoula, and the Missoula County Prosecutor's Office — including retaliatory prosecution following protected First Amendment activity, a warrantless arrest and jailing, cross-jurisdictional off-the-record coordination between law enforcement, and an institutional pattern that Mr. Nuno documented had affected a wide cross-section of individuals over more than a decade.

POST's initial response, sent by Katrina Bolger (Advanced Certified Paralegal) on August 12, 2025, stated that the state's firewalls blocked viewing external links and directed Mr. Nuno to complete a standard POST complaint form. Mr. Nuno complied promptly, had the form notarized, and resubmitted on August 12, 2025, with attachments.
### The Dismissal: Under 24 Hours, No Substantive Review
On August 13, 2025 — **approximately 15 hours after receiving the notarized complaint form** — POST Director Timothy Allred reviewed the submission and Katrina Bolger transmitted the response:

> *"Director Allred has reviewed your complaint information. The majority of the issues that you have outlined are not within POST's purview. Additionally, except in very limited circumstances, POST does not investigate allegations that are over 5 years old, based on policy and procedures adopted by the Council."*

This dismissal — covering a complaint that took hundreds of hours to compile, spans multiple incidents, involves named officers and named institutional actors, and includes documentary evidence of cross-jurisdictional coordination — was completed in less time than it takes to read a comprehensive multi-document submission. The grounds cited were:

1. **Subject matter jurisdiction:** "Not within POST's purview" — applied as a categorical blanket rather than a complaint-by-complaint analysis.
2. **Temporal limitation:** Allegations more than 5 years old are not investigated — applied without any acknowledgment that the complaint described a continuing pattern of conduct, not isolated historical incidents.
### The Correction: Refused and Re-Closed
Mr. Nuno responded on August 13, 2025, with a clarifying submission specifically addressing the jurisdictional mischaracterization. He explicitly noted that the complaint was not about isolated past incidents but about an **ongoing pattern of structural misconduct** involving organizational conflicts of interest between MPD, the YWCA, and prosecutors — conduct that continues to the present day. He provided additional documentation and requested proper referral to another DOJ department if POST's purview was insufficient.

POST did not re-engage substantively. On August 20, 2025, Mr. Nuno followed up again — noting that the complaint had been dismissed "less than 24 hours after submission" — and asked whether POST's dismissal was final. He received no substantive response.

On August 26, 2025, Mr. Nuno submitted a further clarifying statement that included an editorial-format summary of the complaint specifically designed to remove technical complexity and make the core issue unmistakable: this was a systemic, ongoing matter affecting multiple victims over a decade, not a historical individual grievance. He explicitly warned that if he did not receive a response, he would "document the refusal to even attempt a review" and seek oversight through other channels.

On August 27, 2025 — six minutes past 7:00 AM — Katrina Bolger replied:

> *"For the reasons stated in my August 13, 2025, email below, POST will not proceed with an investigation of the allegations and this matter is closed."*

The matter was closed for the final time. The total elapsed time from initial submission to final closure: **16 days.** The number of substantive responses received: **zero.** The issues with POST's characterization of the complaint that Mr. Nuno raised: **never addressed.**
### Analysis: What the POST Response Reveals
Several features of POST's documented response to Mr. Nuno's complaint are analytically significant:

**1. The temporal limitation rule as a structural barrier to pattern documentation.** POST's policy of not investigating allegations over 5 years old is internally coherent if one assumes that misconduct consists of isolated incidents. It is profoundly dysfunctional when applied to a complaint documenting a sustained institutional pattern. Patterns of misconduct are, by definition, only visible over time. A rule that excludes historical incidents from review effectively immunizes the earliest — and often most significant — acts in a pattern, forcing complainants to choose between complaining in real time (before the pattern is visible) or waiting until the pattern is undeniable (by which time the early incidents are excluded).

**2. "Not within POST's purview" applied without analysis.** POST's dismissal does not specify which allegations fall outside its jurisdiction and which might fall within it. POST has clear jurisdiction over officer certification and conduct standards, which include conduct unbecoming, dishonesty, violation of civil rights, and coordination with non-law-enforcement actors to harm civilians. The complaint named specific officers, specific incidents, and specific patterns directly implicating these categories. A legitimate jurisdictional triage would have parsed the complaint and retained the portions within POST's mandate. The categorical dismissal instead — issued in under 24 hours — suggests the complaint was not read in full.

**3. Compliance requirements as delay mechanisms.** POST's initial response — directing Mr. Nuno to use the standard complaint form rather than reading his existing submission — introduced a one-day procedural delay that was then used as the clock for the 15-hour dismissal. The attachment-stripping policy (encrypted or digitally signed files from ProtonMail were automatically stripped by Montana's email systems) further obstructed complete submission of documentation. These are not trivial administrative details; they represent structural friction that selectively disadvantages technologically sophisticated complainants using secure communications tools.

**4. No referral to appropriate agencies.** When Mr. Nuno explicitly asked POST to identify other DOJ departments better equipped to address his complaint, POST did not respond to this request. A functioning accountability system would have — at minimum — pointed a complainant with a serious multi-agency misconduct complaint toward appropriate alternative venues.

## Part IV: The Accountability Pipeline — How Each Level Fails
The architecture of Montana's police accountability system can be understood as a pipeline that processes civilian complaints. At each stage, design features filter out complaints before they can reach meaningful review:

```
INCIDENT → CIVILIAN COMPLAINT FILING → LOCAL OPS INVESTIGATION → 
POLICE COMMISSION REVIEW → POST COMPLAINT → POST CASE STATUS COMMITTEE → 
POST INVESTIGATION → DECERTIFICATION / SANCTION
```

### Stage 1: Complaint Filing (Local Level)
**Design feature:** Complaints routed through accused agency. **Effect:** Severe underreporting (MPD: ~99% below expected UoF baseline). **Evidence:** 4 UoF complaints vs. 505+ documented incidents in 2021-2022 alone.
### Stage 2: OPS Investigation (Local Level)
**Design feature:** Internal affairs investigation by same department. Findings confidential. **Effect:** Low sustain rates; no independent audit possible. **Evidence:** Zero sustained UoF and discrimination findings 2016-2022; records blanket-withheld as "personnel files."
### Stage 3: Police Commission Review (Municipal Level)
**Design feature:** Advisory body, mayoral appointees, quarterly meetings, no enforcement authority, no independent investigative capacity. **Effect:** Non-binding recommendations only. **Evidence:** Chair confirmed on record "we don't tell the police department what to do."
### Stage 4: POST Complaint Filing (State Level)
**Design feature:** Must exhaust employing agency first; standard form required; attachment-stripping policies disrupt documentation. **Effect:** Complainants with complex, multi-document complaints face procedural friction before triage begins. **Evidence:** Mr. Nuno required to resubmit notarized form one day before dismissal.
### Stage 5: POST Case Status Committee Triage (State Level)
**Design feature:** Committee reviews complaint alongside employing agency response; may close without investigation; temporal limitation rule (5+ years = excluded); categorical purview screening. **Effect:** Complex civilian complaints closed in hours without substantive review. **Evidence:** Mr. Nuno's complaint dismissed in under 24 hours, correction refused, matter re-closed without engagement.
### Stage 6: POST Integrity Outcomes (State Level)
**Design feature:** Published outcomes reflect cases that passed all prior filters. **Effect:** Published record consists almost entirely of internally-generated misconduct (drug use, exam cheating, criminal acts); civilian-origin complaints absent. **Evidence:** POST Integrity Reports 2013, published POST data.
### The Result: Accountability Without Function
Each stage of this pipeline operates within its own formal rules and procedures. No individual actor at any stage necessarily acts with malice or conscious intent to suppress legitimate complaints. The OPS investigator follows department policy. The Police Commission chair operates within the scope of Montana Code Annotated §§ 7-32-4151 to 7-32-4164. The POST paralegal applies the Council's adopted temporal limitation rule. The Case Status Committee follows its adopted procedures.

The systemic failure is a **design failure**, not a behavioral one. When each component of a multi-stage oversight system is designed to resolve ambiguity in favor of early closure, protect institutional actors from external scrutiny, and withhold records from public audit, **the aggregate result is a system that cannot hold police accountable to civilians.** That this outcome is consistent across all institutional levels — local, municipal, and state — and that it is empirically documented through both statistical data and primary-source complaint correspondence — makes the design-failure characterization not just plausible but the most parsimonious explanation of the available evidence.

## Part V: The Negligence Standard and What Follows From It
The appropriate framing for these findings is not that every employee in these oversight bodies is malicious, but that their institutions are **negligent in the discharge of the accountability function they were created to perform.** "Negligence" in this context has a specific meaning: the failure to exercise the standard of care that a reasonable accountability body would exercise when presented with a documented, detailed, multi-year misconduct complaint involving named officers, named incidents, and documented patterns.

The standard for a reasonable accountability body would include:

- Reading a complaint before dismissing it
- Parsing which elements fall within and outside jurisdiction before issuing categorical denials
- Responding to corrections when an applicant identifies a factual mischaracterization
- Referring matters outside one's jurisdiction to appropriate bodies, rather than simply closing them
- Applying temporal limitation rules with acknowledgment of continuing-violation doctrine for pattern-of-conduct complaints

POST met **none** of these standards in its handling of Mr. Nuno's August 2025 complaint. MPD's OPS meets **none** of these standards as evidenced by the statistical record. The Police Commission, by the testimony of its own chair, does not purport to meet these standards.

This is institutional negligence — a systematic abdication of the accountability function across every level of the oversight hierarchy. It is not a conspiracy. It is an architecture.

## Part VI: Implications and Next Steps
### For Investigative Journalists
The most independently verifiable story is the use-of-force disparity: **505 UoF incidents documented by MPD in 2021-2022 alone; 4 total UoF complaints filed across 7 years; zero sustained.** Both halves of that figure come from MPD's own published sources. No confidential records are required. The story writes itself.

The secondary story is the POST pipeline: a paralegal reviewed and closed a multi-year, multi-document civil rights complaint in under 15 hours — and then refused to engage with a correction. The correspondence is documented in primary-source email records. Neither story requires anonymous sources. Both are verifiable from publicly available or directly obtained documents.
### For State Legislators
The CRV Committee's current evidence-gathering window makes this the appropriate moment to present these findings. The specific legislative failure points are:

1. **Montana has no independent civilian police review board** at the state level with investigative authority over the most serious misconduct allegations.
2. **POST's temporal limitation rule** categorically excludes pattern-of-conduct complaints from investigation.
3. **The personnel records exemption** is applied by Montana cities in a manner that withholds even aggregate statistical data — a position that arguably exceeds the statutory exemption and should be clarified by the Legislature.
4. **The Police Commission structure** (MCA §§ 7-32-4151 to 7-32-4164) provides advisory authority only; it should be amended to provide independent investigative capacity with limited subpoena power for the most serious complaint categories.
### For the DOJ Civil Rights Division
The documented pattern — zero sustained UoF and discrimination findings over 7 years, a 9.7x racial disparity in low-level arrests, blanket records suppression, and a state oversight body that dismisses complex misconduct complaints in under 24 hours — constitutes the type of statistical and procedural evidence that supported prior DOJ pattern-or-practice investigations, including the 2012 DOJ investigation of Missoula itself. The political constraints on current DOJ enforcement are real, but documentation of this pattern now — in the public record — creates the evidentiary foundation for future enforcement action regardless of the current administration's posture.
