# Remembering When: Missoula Rape Capital Era and Institutional Failures

## The Roots of Missoula's Present Day Institutional Corruption

In 2012 Missoula caught national attention for becoming what was referred to as the "rape capital of the country" when the public became aware of a systemic pattern of discounting and discrediting sexual assault victims, mostly perpetrated by members of the University of Montana's "Griz" football team.

This national attention triggered the US DoJ to launch an exhaustive investigation into the institutional failings that allowed victims to go unprotected and such abhorrent criminal conduct to remain unreported and unpunished.

In 2013-2014 the US DoJ began the process of publishing their findings and securing formal agreements with Missoula law enforcement, prosecutorial agencies, and local community victims advocacy institutions (of which the YWCA was included), and concluded with formally closing the case in 2016.

## Institutional Conflicts of Interest and Board Composition

The YWCA of Missoula's governance structure presents an immediate central red flag: a sitting MPD detective (Connie Brueckner) as a Board member at-large, alongside other government-linked directors. This arrangement threatens core fiduciary duties and undermines the appearance and reality of independence.

The empirical backdrop compounds these concerns. Multiple studies find that police officers perpetrate domestic violence at approximately 2-4 times the rate of the general population, with best estimates near 21%.

## How Does This Get Fixed?

### 1. Governance and Ethics Reform

* Remove active law enforcement officers from the Board of victim advocacy institutions
* Establish an independent ethics and compliance committee
* Adopt and enforce a comprehensive conflict-of-interest policy

### 2. Client Safety, Confidentiality, and Independence

* Implement auditable information governance
* Prohibit staff from issuing court recommendations beyond verified documentation
* Create a dedicated pathway for survivors alleging officer-involved abuse

### 3. Accountability and Redress

* Open an independent investigation into staff misconduct allegations
* Create a survivor grievance process overseen by an external ombudsperson

## Conclusion

Reform is not only possible - it is essential. Removing police from governance, hardening confidentiality, professionalizing advocacy boundaries, and subjecting operations to independent oversight are the minimum steps required to restore trust.
